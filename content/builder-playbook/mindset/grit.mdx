# GRIT

Dr. Craig Manning spent years studying what actually predicts long-term success — not in a lab, but with elite performers across sports, sales, and high-performance fields. His findings are counterintuitive and worth understanding deeply.

<Accordion title="Predictors of Long-Term Success">
| Factor | Weight |
|--------|--------|
| Determination | **76%** |
| Confidence | 8% |
| Emotional Stability | 7% |
| Job Functionality | 5% |
| Character | 4% |

The numbers make a point: determination is not one of several factors — it's almost the whole game. Confidence, emotional stability, and all the rest matter at the margins. Grit is the engine.

This is useful data when you're in a rough week. The most talented rep in your region without determination will lose to the most stubborn rep with it. Every time, over the long run.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="What Determination Actually Is">
Determination isn't willpower in the moment. It's the accumulated habit of doing hard things consistently, even when motivation is absent.

The rep who knocks when it's hot, books when they're tired, and shows up on Saturday when every instinct says don't — that rep is building determination the same way a weightlifter builds muscle. Through resistance.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Six Steps to Strengthen Willpower">
These aren't suggestions. They're disciplines that directly affect your mental capacity to persist.

### 1. Get Healthy
Your body is the hardware your mindset runs on. Chronic fatigue, poor nutrition, and physical inactivity all reduce mental durability. You cannot have elite willpower in a depleted body.

### 2. Sleep 7.5-9 Hours
Sleep is not optional and not a sign of weakness. Sleep debt directly impairs decision-making, emotional regulation, and persistence. The rep who runs on 5 hours is playing with a handicap they can't see.

### 3. Eat 5 Fruits and Vegetables Daily
Blood sugar stability affects mood and decision quality throughout the day. The afternoon energy crash is often a nutrition issue, not a motivation issue.

### 4. Move
Physical movement — especially in the morning — primes the brain for focus and stress tolerance. A workout before a door shift changes what you're capable of at 6pm.

### 5. Practice Gratitude
Gratitude is not a soft habit. It actively interrupts the negativity bias that makes doors feel heavier than they are. Morning gratitude practice shifts your baseline emotional state.

### 6. Think Intentionally (DMP Work)
See the **Definite Major Purpose** module. Intentional thought about who you're becoming and what you're building creates internal alignment that sustains effort when external motivation fails.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Identify and Leverage Your Strengths">
You have things you're already good at. Know what they are, name them explicitly, and find ways to deploy them more often. Performance in your zones of strength builds confidence that carries into harder terrain.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Identify and Neutralize Your Weaknesses">
Weaknesses don't disappear by ignoring them — they ambush you. Name yours. Build a system that minimizes their impact. If you're bad at mornings, structure accountability. If you're inconsistent with follow-up, build a forcing function.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="ACT">
Knowing isn't enough. Reading this isn't enough. The step from knowledge to performance is action, taken now, in the real world, even imperfectly.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Surround Yourself with the Right People">
This is the one step that multiplies all the others. Your environment shapes your standard. The people you spend the most time with set the floor for your effort, your attitude, and your expectation of what's possible.

Choose accordingly.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="The Grit Summary">
> Determination explains 76% of long-term success. Determination is built through consistent action under resistance. Consistent action is sustained by physical health, sleep, nutrition, movement, gratitude, and intentional thought. The rep who takes this seriously will outperform nearly everyone who doesn't.
</Accordion>
