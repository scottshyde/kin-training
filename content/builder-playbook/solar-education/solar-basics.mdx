# Solar Education Basics for Sales Leaders

<Lead>You don't need to be an engineer — but you need to know the fundamentals well enough to coach setters on technical questions, answer customer questions with confidence, evaluate proposal quality, and position KIN's advantages intelligently.</Lead>

<GoldRule />

<Accordion title="How Solar Actually Works">

<Steps>
<Step number={1} title="Panels Capture Sunlight">Photons hit silicon, electrons get excited, DC power is produced.</Step>
<Step number={2} title="Inverter Converts to AC">Direct current becomes alternating current for the home.</Step>
<Step number={3} title="Power Goes to Your Home First">Appliances use solar power immediately.</Step>
<Step number={4} title="Excess Goes to the Grid">Extra power flows back to SDGE — they credit you.</Step>
<Step number={5} title="At Night, You Pull from Grid">When the sun goes down, you use grid power.</Step>
<Step number={6} title="Net Metering Credits You">SDGE tallies consumption vs. production at the end of the billing cycle.</Step>
</Steps>

<KeyTerm term="What the Customer Experiences">Day: home runs on solar, bill drops. Night: draws from grid, uses credits. Month: bill shows net consumption. Year: seasonal variation — more production in summer, less in winter.</KeyTerm>

</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Six Key Concepts Every Leader Must Know">

<StatBlock stats={[
  { value: "kWh", label: "How Electricity Is Measured" },

  { value: "PPA/LOAN/$", label: "Three Financing Paths" },
  { value: "5-8%", label: "Annual SDGE Rate Increases" }

<Steps>
<Step number={1} title="kWh (Kilowatt-Hour)">

1 kW used for 1 hour = 1 kWh. SDGE charges by kWh. System size is measured in kW (8kW = can produce 8 kilowatts at peak).

<PullQuote>"Your bill shows you use 1,200 kWh per month. Our system will produce about 1,000 kWh per month. That's 80% offset, which means 80% savings."</PullQuote>

</Step>

<Step number={2} title="Net Metering">

Excess solar goes back to the grid. SDGE credits you. You build a "bank" of credits to use later (summer overproduction covers winter shortfall).

<PullQuote>"In summer, you produce more than you use. That excess goes back to SDGE, and they credit you. In winter, when you use more, you use those credits. That's year-round benefit."</PullQuote>

</Step>

<Step number={3} title="PPA vs. Loan vs. Cash">

| Option | Ownership | Down Payment | Tax Credit | Best For |
|--------|-----------|-------------|------------|----------|
| **PPA** | KIN owns it | $0 | No | Budget-conscious, simple |
| **Loan** | You own it | $0-minimal | Yes (30%) | Equity builders, tax benefit |
| **Cash** | You own it | Full price | Yes (30%) | Maximum long-term savings |

<KeyTerm term="Your Job">Match the customer to the right option. Each has different financing, tax implications, and long-term value.</KeyTerm>

</Step>

<Step number={4} title="System Size vs. Consumption">

System size is measured in kW. Consumption is measured in kWh/month from their bill. System should offset 75-100% of consumption.

<PullQuote>"Your house uses 1,500 kWh per month. An 8kW system will produce about 1,200 kWh per month in San Diego. That's 80% offset — perfect for you."</PullQuote>

</Step>

<Step number={5} title="SDGE Rate Increases">

5-8% annual increases historically. Not slowing down — energy demand is rising. Solar locks in savings. ROI improves as utility rates rise.

<PullQuote>"SDGE is increasing rates 18% this year. Over 25 years, your rates will probably triple. With our PPA, your rate is locked at 3.5% increase annually. That difference is your long-term savings."</PullQuote>

</Step>

<Step number={6} title="Battery Storage">

Stores excess solar production. Allows solar power at night. Provides backup during outages. Expensive upfront ($8,000-15,000).

<PullQuote>"Most customers start with solar only. Some add batteries for maximum savings and backup power. It depends on their priorities and budget."</PullQuote>

</Step>
</Steps>

</Accordion>

<Accordion title="SDGE-Specific Knowledge">

<Steps>
<Step number={1} title="Interconnection Timeline">

System installed and inspected → KIN submits interconnection request → SDGE reviews (2-6 weeks) → SDGE approves and installs new meter → System goes live.

<Callout type="tip">
Manage expectations. SDGE timelines vary. Reassure customers during the waiting period. Bills show solar offset immediately after go-live.
</Callout>
</Step>

<Step number={2} title="NEM 3.0 (Net Energy Metering 3.0)">

California modified net metering rules effective 2023. Reduces credit for excess solar sent to grid. Affects system design — shouldn't go over 100% offset. Still makes solar economically viable.

<PullQuote>"California changed net metering rules recently. Solar is still an amazing investment, but it changed how we calculate the credit for excess power. Let me show you how this impacts your specific numbers."</PullQuote>

</Step>

<Step number={3} title="Time of Use (TOU) Rates">

SDGE charges different rates at different times. Peak hours (4-9 PM) = highest. Solar production peaks midday when rates are lower. Evening consumption isn't covered by solar alone.

<KeyTerm term="Battery Value">This is where batteries become compelling — store peak-production power for peak-rate evening use.</KeyTerm>

</Step>
</Steps>

</Accordion>

|------|------|------|

| **Hybrid** | Handles solar + battery | More complex, required for battery systems |

<KeyTerm term="Your Role">Know what KIN uses and why it's better than cheap alternatives. Understand the tradeoffs.</KeyTerm>

<Accordion title="Common Technical Questions">

<Callout type="important" title="KNOW THESE COLD">
Your team will bring these to you. Have answers ready.
</Callout>

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| **"Do panels work on cloudy days?"** | Yes — about 25-50% output. San Diego's sun makes annual production excellent. |
| **"What about extreme heat?"** | Panels produce ~10% less at 110°F. Our panels have good temperature ratings. |
| **"Can I expand later?"** | Yes, though it's easier to design the right size upfront. Expansions require new permits. |
| **"What if my roof needs replacing?"** | We assess roof condition first. If it's old, replace the roof first, then install solar on the new roof. |

</Accordion>
