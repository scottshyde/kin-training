# Standards

Standards aren't restrictions — they're the conditions under which serious people can do serious work together. Knowing the standard protects everyone: the team, the rep, and the homeowners we're serving.

<Accordion title="Two-Strike Policy">
KIN Home operates on a two-strike manner for conduct violations. This means the first serious lapse is addressed directly. The second ends the relationship.

This isn't punitive — it's a signal that we take both our team culture and our reputation in the field seriously.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Meeting Standards">
Meetings are the cadence of the team. They're where accountability happens, where skills develop, and where culture is reinforced.

**Attendance is required.** "Attendance" comes from the Latin *attendere* — to tend to. You can't tend to something you're not present for.

**Rules for meetings:**
- Nothing scheduled during meetings
- No phones or screens during meetings
- New reps attend every meeting for the first 10 weeks — no exceptions
- On time, focused, and prepared
- Handwritten notes (retention is 40% higher than typed)
- No call/no show to two meetings = you're off the team

**Acceptable absences** (must be communicated in advance):
- Funeral or immediate family death
- Giving birth
- Documented family emergency
- Pre-arranged vacation (2 weeks notice required)

Everything else is expected attendance.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Minimum Closer Standards">
These benchmarks apply to reps receiving set leads:

| Metric | Minimum Standard |
|--------|-----------------|
| Closing % | ≥40% across 10+ deals |
| Throughput | >80% |

A rep receiving leads and not meeting these standards is a drag on both the setter's pipeline and the team's economics. These are minimums, not targets.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="People of Character (PPW)">
The shorthand is **PPW** — People of Character. This is the standard for who gets to be on this team and represent KIN in the field.

People of Character:
- Do what they say they're going to do
- Tell the truth, including about their numbers
- Treat homeowners with genuine respect
- Show up prepared
- Own their mistakes without blame-shifting
- Compete hard without tearing others down

This isn't idealism — it's a practical filter. Reps without character create problems that cost the team more than their deals are worth. We'd rather have fewer people who are trustworthy than more people who aren't.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="A Note on Standards and Self-Respect">
Every standard on this list is also an act of self-respect. The rep who shows up on time, does the Power Hour, hits their door count, and tells the truth is building something — not just a paycheck, but a professional identity.

The reps who've built real careers in this industry did it by holding themselves to a standard when no one was watching. That's the version of standards that matters.
</Accordion>
