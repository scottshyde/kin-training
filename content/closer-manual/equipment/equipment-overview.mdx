### Key Specifications

<StatBlock stats={[
  { value: "330-430W", label: "Typical residential panel wattage" },

  { value: "25yr", label: "Linear degradation warranty" },

<KeyTerm term="Temperature Coefficient">How much output decreases in heat. Range: -0.25% to -0.40% per °C above 25°C. San Diego systems produce ~10% less on 110° days vs. 75° days. Better panels have lower coefficients.</KeyTerm>

### Panel Tiers

**Tier 1 (Premium):** LG, Sunpower, Panasonic — higher cost, better efficiency, proven longevity.

**Tier 2 (Mid-Range):** Canadian Solar, JinkoSolar, JA Solar — good balance of cost and performance.

**Tier 3 (Budget):** Avoid. Higher failure rates, warranty risk, damages customer satisfaction.

<PullQuote>"We use 400W panels with 20.2% efficiency and a 0.37% negative temperature coefficient. Even on hot San Diego days, you're getting excellent performance. Backed by a 25-year warranty — best in the industry."</PullQuote>

### Monocrystalline vs. Polycrystalline

<Callout type="tip">
All modern residential installs use **monocrystalline** — single crystal, higher efficiency (18-22%), better low-light performance, sleeker look, longer lifespan. Polycrystalline (15-17%) is becoming obsolete.
</Callout>

</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Inverters">

### Two Types

<Steps>
<Step number={1} title="String Inverters (Most Common)">
All panels wired together in "strings" — one central inverter converts all DC to AC. **Less expensive**, simpler to install. Drawback: if one panel has shade, entire string is affected.

**Best for:** Roofs with good uniform sun exposure, budget-conscious customers.
</Step>

<Step number={2} title="Micro-Inverters (Premium)">
Each panel gets its own tiny inverter. Independent optimization and monitoring. **Better for complex roofs** with multiple angles or shading. 30-50% higher cost.

**Best for:** Complex roofs, significant shading, customers who value max production.
</Step>
</Steps>

<Accordion title="Batteries Under NEM 3.0: Why Every System Needs One">

<Callout type="important" title="THE NEW REALITY">
NEM 3.0 changed everything. Export credits dropped roughly **75%**. Without a battery, you're giving solar away for almost nothing during the day and buying it back at peak rates in the evening.
</Callout>

<PullQuote>"Under the new rules in California, sending solar back to the grid doesn't pay you much at all anymore. That's why every system we install now includes battery storage. You store your solar during the day and use it in the evening when rates are highest. That's how you actually save money under the new structure."</PullQuote>

</Accordion>

<GoldRule />

<Accordion title="Grid Arbitrage">

<KeyTerm term="Grid Arbitrage">The battery pays for itself by shifting energy from cheap production hours to expensive consumption hours.</KeyTerm>

<Steps>
<Step number={1} title="Solar Produces During the Day">
Electricity rates are low — your production cost is $0.00.
</Step>
<Step number={2} title="Battery Stores Instead of Exporting">
No more giving away power for pennies.
</Step>
<Step number={3} title="Discharge During Peak (4-9pm)">
SDGE charges $0.50+/kWh. Every kWh your battery shifts saves that full amount.
</Step>
<Step number={4} title="The Spread = Your Savings">
Off-peak to peak spread in San Diego runs **$0.30-$0.50+** per kWh. Over a year, that's thousands.
</Step>
</Steps>

<PullQuote>"Think of your battery like a savings account for electricity. You fill it up when power is cheap — or free from your solar — and spend it when power is the most expensive. That's where your real savings come from."</PullQuote>

</Accordion>

<GoldRule />

<Accordion title="Non-Export Mode">

<KeyTerm term="Non-Export">Your system never sends power back to the grid. Every kWh stays in your home or your battery.</KeyTerm>

**Why it works under NEM 3.0:** Export credits are so low that self-consumption is worth **4-5x more** than exporting.

<Steps>
<Step number={1} title="Solar Produces → Home Uses What It Needs">
Direct consumption first.
</Step>
<Step number={2} title="Excess Charges the Battery">
Everything surplus goes to storage.
</Step>
<Step number={3} title="Battery Full → System Throttles">
No export. No giving it away.
</Step>
<Step number={4} title="Evening → Battery Discharges at Peak">
Maximum value from every kWh.
</Step>
</Steps>

<PullQuote>"We set your system to keep every bit of power you produce. Nothing goes back to the grid. You use it or you store it. No giving it away for pennies."</PullQuote>

</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Partial Backup">

<Callout type="insight">
One battery powering your **whole** house during an outage — that's not how it works. Partial backup covers only your **critical circuits**.
</Callout>

**What stays on:** Refrigerator, lights in key rooms, Wi-Fi router, phone charging, garage door.

**What doesn't:** HVAC, electric stove, pool pump, EV charger, washer/dryer.

<KeyTerm term="Critical Loads Panel">Wired separately during installation. Only designated circuits get battery power in an outage. One battery (10-15 kWh) keeps critical loads running **12-24+ hours**.</KeyTerm>

<PullQuote>"Your battery keeps the essentials running during an outage — fridge, lights, Wi-Fi, devices. We wire a critical loads panel so the important things stay on. One battery can keep those running a full day or more."</PullQuote>

</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Common Customer Questions">

### "How long do solar panels last?"

<PullQuote>"Panels are designed to produce at 80% of rated capacity after 25 years. Most installations from 20+ years ago are still producing great. They essentially last forever — they just degrade very slowly."</PullQuote>

### "What happens if a panel breaks?"

<PullQuote>"Covered by our 25-year warranty. We replace it at no cost. But panels are incredibly durable — tested to withstand hail, extreme wind, and temperature swings. Failures are extremely rare."</PullQuote>

### "Do panels work on cloudy days?"

<PullQuote>"Yes — about 25-50% of rated output compared to sunny days. San Diego has so many sunny days that even with a few cloudy ones, your annual production is excellent."</PullQuote>

### "What about when it's really hot?"

<PullQuote>"Solar panels are actually less efficient when very hot — counterintuitive, right? On a 110° day, maybe 10-15% less than a 75° day. But our panels have excellent temperature coefficients that minimize the loss."</PullQuote>
