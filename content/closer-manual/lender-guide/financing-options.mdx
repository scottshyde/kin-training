# Financing Options: PPA, Loan, Lease, and Cash

## The Four Paths to Solar Ownership

Every solar customer falls into one of four financing categories. Your job as a closer is to:
1. Understand each option deeply
2. Guide the customer to the best choice based on their situation
3. Present it with confidence so they feel the decision is right

## Option 1: PPA (Power Purchase Agreement)

### What is a PPA?

A PPA is essentially a "solar lease-to-own" model where:
- **KIN finances and owns the system**
- **Customer pays per kWh used** (like a utility bill)
- **No money down** - $0 upfront investment
- **25-year agreement** - Customer is locked in for the full period
- **KIN maintains the system** - All maintenance and repairs included

### How the Economics Work

**Example PPA:**
- Customer's current SDGE bill: $150/month ($1,800/year)
- 8kW system offsets 80% of bill (~$1,440/year)
- KIN PPA rate: $0.12/kWh (customer's rate, locked in)
- Customer's solar payment: ~$115/month
- Customer's remaining SDGE bill: ~$35/month
- **Total savings:** $35/month vs. baseline ($420/year)

### Key PPA Features

**Rate Escalator:**
- PPA rate increases 3.5% annually
- Year 1: $0.12/kWh
- Year 5: $0.14/kWh
- Year 10: $0.17/kWh
- Year 25: $0.35/kWh

**Why the escalator matters:**
- Locks customer into savings even as SDGE rates increase
- SDGE increases 5-8% annually (historically)
- 3.5% PPA escalator = 2-4% annual savings growth
- Long-term this is huge savings vs. staying with SDGE

**Credit Requirements:**
- Minimum credit score: ~650 (some variation)
- Payment history matters more than total debt
- No maximum credit score (great credit = same terms)

**Ownership:**
- KIN owns the system
- Customer has "rights to use" the solar power for 25 years
- At end of 25 years: Customer can remove system, replace system, or extend agreement

**Maintenance:**
- 100% covered by KIN
- Zero maintenance cost to customer
- If panel fails, KIN replaces it
- If inverter fails, KIN replaces it
- 25-year warranty = zero worry

### Who Should Choose PPA

**Perfect PPA Candidates:**
- **Credit-constrained:** Can't get loan approval
- **No cash:** Don't have savings for down payment
- **Want simplicity:** Prefer paying a bill vs. managing investments
- **Conservative:** Want predictable costs with no surprises
- **Maintenance-averse:** Don't want to think about system upkeep
- **Renters approaching ownership:** Might move in 5-7 years (but 25-year agreement is sticky)

**Not ideal for:**
- People planning to sell home in 5 years (can be resolved)
- High earners wanting tax credits (they should do loan)
- People wanting to own asset (should do loan/cash)

### PPA Word Track

**Opening:**
> "You've got four options for solar. Let me walk you through each so you understand which is best for you.
>
> Option 1 is our PPA, which stands for Power Purchase Agreement. Think of it like a solar utility bill. KIN finances and owns the system, maintains it, and you simply pay us for the power you use.
>
> Here's why some customers love it: Zero dollars down. No maintenance. Your payment is predictable and locked in."

**The Pitch:**
> "Your SDGE bill is $150/month. With our PPA, your solar payment will be about $115/month. That's $35/month savings immediately, and the savings grow every year as SDGE's rates increase faster than our 3.5% escalator.
>
> Plus, there's zero risk. If anything ever breaks, we fix it. You're not responsible for maintenance. You just pay your bill and enjoy clean energy."

**Closing the PPA:**
> "You need to have at least a 650 credit score, which I'm sure you do. [Confirm] Great. Then the PPA is a fantastic option - no money down, locked-in savings, and zero maintenance. You'll love the simplicity."

### PPA Objections & Responses

**Objection: "I don't like being tied to a 25-year agreement"**

Response:
> "I understand - it's a long time. But here's the thing: You're already tied to SDGE. The only difference is now you're paying us a lower rate. In fact, you can move homes - the new homeowner can take over the agreement, or you can remove the system if you'd like.
>
> Most customers don't think of it as being tied down. They think of it as locking in low rates for 25 years. That's actually a great position to be in."

**Objection: "What if I want to remove the system later?"**

Response:
> "You can. You own your home - the system is on your roof. If at any point you want to remove it, you can. We'd remove it, restore your roof, and the agreement ends. Most customers never want to remove it, but the option is always there."

**Objection: "The rate going up 3.5% every year bugs me"**

Response:
> "I hear you. But let me put it in perspective: SDGE's rate increases 5-8% every year on average. You're locking in 3.5%. So your savings actually grow every year.
>
> Year 1, you save $35/month. By year 5, you'll save $55/month. By year 10, you'll save $90/month. The escalator is actually your friend."

**Objection: "What if I can't get approved?"**

Response:
> "You need a 650 credit score minimum. Do you think you're above that? [Listen] Then you should be fine. We'll run the credit check during the approval process. If for some reason you don't qualify, we can look at other options like a loan or cash."

## Option 2: Loan (Solar Loan / Home Equity Loan / HELOC)

### What is a Loan?

Customer finances the purchase of the system with:
- **Upfront cost:** Full system price (~$2-3 per watt after incentives)
- **Monthly payment:** Usually 7-15 years
- **Ownership:** Customer owns the system immediately
- **Tax credit:** Customer gets 30% federal ITC
- **Maintenance:** Customer responsible (or can buy maintenance plan)

### How the Economics Work

**Example Loan:**
- System cost: $20,000
- Federal tax credit (30%): -$6,000
- Net cost: $14,000
- 10-year loan at 7% APR: ~$160/month
- System produces: ~$1,800/year in SDGE offset
- **Net savings Year 1:** ~$18/month ($1,800 - $1,920 payments)
- **Breakeven:** Year 5-6 (when cumulative tax credit + savings exceed payments)
- **Payoff Year 10:** System paid for, then 15+ years of free power

### Key Loan Features

**Ownership:**
- Customer owns system immediately
- Full responsibility for maintenance (can buy warranty)
- Can sell home with system (transferable)
- No "end date" - system is yours forever

**Tax Credit:**
- 30% federal ITC taken first year
- Worth $6,000 on $20,000 system
- Reduces loan amount or comes back as tax refund
- Increases savings significantly

**Interest Rates:**
- Home equity line of credit: 7-9% (uses home equity)
- Personal solar loan: 5-11% (depends on lender and credit)
- Cash-out refinance: Similar to home equity line

**Loan Terms:**
- 7 years: Higher payment, faster payoff
- 10 years: Balanced payment and timeline
- 15 years: Lower payment, longer payoff
- Longer terms = more interest paid

**Monthly Payment Comparison:**
- System cost: $20,000 (after tax credit applied)
- 7-year loan at 7%: ~$283/month
- 10-year loan at 7%: ~$235/month
- 15-year loan at 7%: ~$199/month

### Who Should Choose a Loan

**Perfect Loan Candidates:**
- **Good credit:** 700+ credit score with good history
- **Long-term homeowners:** Planning to stay 7+ years
- **Tax benefit seekers:** Want to use 30% federal credit
- **Ownership-focused:** Want to own the asset
- **Stable income:** Can handle monthly payment long-term
- **Home equity available:** Have equity to borrow against (if using HELOC)

**Less ideal for:**
- Planning to move in 3-5 years (though transferable)
- Limited credit history
- Unstable income
- No home equity

### Loan Word Track

**Opening:**
> "Option 2 is a loan. You finance the system and own it immediately. You get the system for the lowest long-term cost and you own the asset.
>
> Here's why some customers love it: You own the system. You get the 30% federal tax credit. And after you pay it off, you've got 15+ years of free power."

**The Pitch:**
> "Your system costs $20,000. But the government gives you a 30% tax credit - that's $6,000 back in your pocket. So your net cost is $14,000. We can finance that for you.
>
> A 10-year loan on $14,000 is about $160/month. Your solar bill today is $150/month. So you're paying $10 more per month to own a system that produces $150+ in power. Plus, you're getting $6,000 in tax credits.
>
> Year 1 break-even, and then you own a system that produces free power for the next 15+ years. That's incredible value."

**Closing the Loan:**
> "With a loan, you own the system. You get the tax credit. And you build equity in a solar asset. If you ever sell your home, the new owner assumes the loan or pays it off, and you keep the equity you built.
>
> This is the financing method that builds the most wealth long-term."

### Loan Objections & Responses

**Objection: "I don't want another monthly payment"**

Response:
> "I understand - we all have enough payments. But here's what's different: This payment goes toward something you own. Every payment you make builds equity in your system.
>
> Plus, your SDGE bill goes down by the same amount. So in many cases, it's a wash - you're just redirecting the money you already pay SDGE toward something you now own.
>
> And after 10 years, that payment goes away, and you've got free solar power for the next 15+ years."

**Objection: "What if the system breaks and I have to pay for repairs?"**

Response:
> "Great question. We include 25-year warranties on everything - panels, inverter, workmanship, performance. If anything breaks, we fix it at no cost.
>
> You can also add a maintenance plan if you want extra peace of mind. For about $25/month, we handle all inspections and maintenance. But even without it, your warranty covers everything."

**Objection: "What's my credit score need to be?"**

Response:
> "We typically want to see 700+ for the best rates. With excellent credit, you might get 5-6% APR. Good credit gets you 6-7%. If you're below 700, we can still work with you, but the rate might be higher.
>
> What's your credit score situation?"

**Objection: "Will this affect my ability to get a mortgage or other loans?"**

Response:
> "It will show up as a mortgage/line of credit on your credit report. But here's the thing: Lenders see solar loans as very low-risk because the system generates income (your savings) that covers the payment.
>
> In most cases, the solar loan actually helps your credit profile because it's a secured loan with a strong payment history. We've never had a customer tell us it hurt their ability to get other loans."

**Objection: "I'd rather wait until I have cash to pay in full"**

Response:
> "That's understandable if you're saving up. But here's the thing: While you're saving, your SDGE bill keeps going up. Every month you wait, you're missing out on savings.
>
> If you wait 5 years to have $20,000 cash, you've paid an extra $10,000+ in SDGE bills. You could have financed, started saving immediately, and be way ahead.
>
> Plus, the solar incentives - including the 30% tax credit - might change or shrink over time. Locking it in now is actually the smarter financial move."

## Option 3: Cash Purchase

### What is a Cash Purchase?

Customer pays the full system cost upfront in cash:
- **Cost:** Full system price (~$2-3 per watt after incentives)
- **Ownership:** Immediate and complete
- **Tax credit:** 30% federal ITC
- **Savings:** Immediate and complete
- **No financing costs:** No interest, no monthly payments

### How the Economics Work

**Example Cash Purchase:**
- System cost: $20,000
- Federal tax credit (30%): -$6,000
- Net out-of-pocket: $14,000
- System produces: ~$1,800/year offset
- **Year 1 return:** $1,800 (+ $6,000 tax credit = $7,800 total benefit)
- **ROI:** ~54% Year 1, then 12.9% annually
- **Breakeven:** Year 7-8
- **25-year savings:** $45,000+

### Key Cash Purchase Features

**Ownership:**
- Immediate full ownership
- Zero monthly payments
- Zero debt
- Can refinance/borrow against system if needed

**Tax Credit:**
- Get full 30% federal ITC
- $6,000 on typical residential system
- No interest payments eating into benefits
- Maximize return on investment

**Break-Even:**
- Shortest break-even: 7-8 years
- Longest ROI timeline, but highest returns
- 25+ years of free power after break-even

**Return on Investment:**
- Stable 12-15% annual return after break-even
- Better than stock market averages historically
- Protected by 25-year warranties

### Who Should Choose Cash

**Perfect Cash Candidates:**
- **Significant liquid savings:** Can pay $15,000-$25,000 without impacting emergency fund
- **Long-term homeowners:** Planning to stay 7+ years (ideally 10+)
- **Wealth-builders:** Looking for smart investment
- **Maximum savings seekers:** Want the absolute lowest long-term cost
- **Debt-averse:** Don't like monthly payments
- **Early retirement planners:** Solar income helps fund retirement

**Less ideal for:**
- Minimal emergency fund
- Planning to move within 7-8 years
- Need liquidity for other investments

### Cash Purchase Word Track

**Opening:**
> "Option 3 is a cash purchase. You pay in full today, you own the system completely, and you eliminate your SDGE bill permanently.
>
> This is our best-case scenario for customer wealth-building. You invest once, and then you get 25+ years of savings."

**The Pitch:**
> "Your system costs $20,000. You pay that in cash. The government gives you a $6,000 tax credit. So your net cost is $14,000.
>
> That system produces $1,800 in SDGE offset every year. Year 7, your $14,000 investment is fully paid back. Then for the next 18 years, you're getting $1,800/year in free power.
>
> Over 25 years, you're saving $45,000+. That's a 320% return on investment. And that's conservative - doesn't even count SDGE rate increases.
>
> Show me another investment that guarantees 12-15% annual returns with zero risk."

**Closing the Cash Sale:**
> "This is the path I'd choose for my own home. Own it outright, zero monthly payments, and enjoy decades of free power. It's the best long-term wealth building."

### Cash Purchase Objections & Responses

**Objection: "I don't have $20,000 lying around"**

Response:
> "Totally understand - most people don't keep that much liquid. That's why we have financing options. But if you do have it, or if you're thinking about it from an investment perspective, cash is the ultimate move.
>
> Some customers use home equity lines of credit to fund a cash purchase. They get the cash purchase benefit (full ownership, full tax credit) but with financing flexibility."

**Objection: "That's a lot of money tied up"**

Response:
> "It is upfront. But here's the math: Instead of that money sitting in a bank earning 0.5% interest, it's in solar earning 12-15% annually. It's essentially a fixed investment that generates returns guaranteed by your electricity savings.
>
> Plus, your money isn't really "tied up" - you could refinance against it, take out a home equity loan, or even remove the system if needed. But in reality, you won't want to because the returns are so good."

**Objection: "What if I need that cash for an emergency?"**

Response:
> "That's a valid concern. Which is why we usually recommend cash buyers have a solid emergency fund separate from solar investment money.
>
> If you're looking at cash but worried about liquidity, financing is actually a better option. You maintain your cash reserves and make monthly payments. That might be a smarter fit for you."

## Option 4: Lease

### What is a Lease?

Similar to PPA but with important differences:
- **No ownership:** Lessor owns system
- **Fixed monthly payment:** Same payment every month (no escalator)
- **Maintenance included:** Company maintains system
- **Shorter commitment:** Sometimes 10-20 years
- **Buy-out option:** Often can purchase system during lease

### Key Lease Features

**Monthly Payment:**
- Fixed amount (typically $50-150/month)
- No annual increase
- Predictable cost forever
- Might be lower than PPA

**Ownership Buyout:**
- Option to purchase system mid-lease (Year 5-10)
- Purchase price typically 50-70% of original cost
- Good for customers who fall in love with ownership

**Simplicity:**
- Similar to PPA - pay bill, company maintains
- No ownership responsibility
- No tax credits (company claims them)
- Good for hands-off customers

**End of Lease:**
- Company removes system or new lease
- System generally doesn't have residual value to leasee

### Who Should Choose Lease

- **Simplicity seekers:** Just want solar without complexity
- **Budget-limited:** Want lowest monthly payment
- **Short-term homeowners:** Planning to move in 10-12 years
- **Hands-off:** Don't want to think about system ownership

### Lease Word Track

> "Option 4 is a lease. Very similar to the PPA, but with a fixed monthly payment that never increases. You pay a flat $85/month, we maintain the system, and you get clean energy.
>
> Lease is great if you want the absolute simplest option and don't mind that we own the system. The downside is you don't get the tax credit and you don't build ownership equity.
>
> Most customers prefer the PPA or loan because they get more long-term benefit, but lease is a solid option if simplicity is your priority."

## How to Guide Customers to Their Best Option

### The Decision Tree

**Question 1: "How long are you planning to stay in the home?"**
- Less than 5 years → Lean toward Lease/PPA
- 5-10 years → PPA or Loan
- 10+ years → Loan or Cash

**Question 2: "Do you have liquid cash available?"**
- No/Limited → PPA or Loan
- Yes ($20,000+) → Cash (if committed to long-term stay)

**Question 3: "What matters more - lowest payment or lowest long-term cost?"**
- Lowest payment → PPA or Lease
- Lowest total cost → Loan or Cash

**Question 4: "How's your credit?"**
- Below 650 → PPA only (no loan/cash requirement)
- 650-700 → PPA or Loan
- 700+ → Any option works

**Question 5: "Do you want to own the system?"**
- No, just want energy → PPA or Lease
- Yes → Loan or Cash

### The Recommendation Framework

**For most customers, your recommendation should be:**

1. **First choice:** Loan
   - Good balance of ownership and affordability
   - Tax credit benefit is substantial
   - Medium-term payoff (10 years)
   - 12-15% annual return is excellent

2. **Second choice:** Cash (if they have it)
   - Maximum long-term wealth building
   - 25-year free power after payoff
   - 54% Year 1 ROI + ongoing benefits

3. **Third choice:** PPA
   - No money down, simple
   - Good for credit-constrained
   - Solid savings with zero maintenance

4. **Fourth choice:** Lease
   - Only if absolutely want simplicity
   - Lowest monthly payment
   - Least long-term value

### Your Recommendation Word Track

> "Based on what you've told me, here's what I'd recommend:
>
> You're planning to stay in the home 10+ years, so you're a great candidate for a loan. You'll own the system, get the $6,000 tax credit, and break even in about 7-8 years. Then you've got 15+ years of free power.
>
> The payment is about $160/month, which is less than your current SDGE bill in most months. And you're building equity in a solar asset.
>
> The PPA is also a solid option if you want zero upfront cost and don't want to own the system. But given your situation, I'd recommend the loan. You're going to come out way ahead."

## Key Takeaways

1. **PPA:** Zero down, predictable payment, includes maintenance, best for credit-constrained
2. **Loan:** Own immediately, get tax credit, build equity, best for most customers
3. **Cash:** Best long-term wealth building, 54% Year 1 ROI, for long-term owners
4. **Lease:** Simplest option, lowest payment, least long-term value

Your role: Understand all four deeply, guide customer to best choice, present with confidence.

When you can explain the four options clearly and help customers see which is best for **their** situation, you've earned the right to close the deal.
