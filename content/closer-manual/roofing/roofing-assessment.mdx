# Roofing Assessment for Solar Installation

Solar panels live on your roof for 25+ years. Before installing a system, we need to ensure the roof can support that weight and won't need major work during the system's lifespan.

<Accordion title="Why Roof Assessment Matters">
Solar panels live on your roof for 25+ years. Before we install a system, we need to ensure the roof can support that weight and that it won't need major work during the system's lifespan. A roof assessment isn't just a technical requirement - it's a key selling point.

> **The Reality:** Most roofs that need replacing before solar will need replacing during the solar system's lifespan. A smart closer addresses this proactively and positions it as part of the value package, not a problem.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="The Four Key Roof Assessment Areas">
### 1. Roof Age: The Timeline Factor

**Why it matters:**
- Standard asphalt shingle roof lifespan: 15-25 years
- If your roof is 18 years old, it has maybe 5-7 years left
- Solar system lasts 25 years
- You don't want customers replacing the roof in Year 8 of their solar system

**How to determine roof age:**
- Ask the customer: "When was your roof replaced?"
- Check documentation: Look for roofing permits on home documents
- Visual inspection: Look at shingles - curled, cracked, missing = old roof
- Satellite imagery: Some companies can estimate from Google Earth photos

**Age classifications:**
- **0-5 years old:** Excellent - system will outlast the roof replacement cycle
- **5-10 years old:** Good - system and roof will age together
- **10-15 years old:** Acceptable - roof has 10+ years left, system fits
- **15-20 years old:** Concerning - roof might need replacement during system lifespan
- **20+ years old:** Problematic - roof likely needs replacement soon

**Your Assessment Script:**
> "How old is your roof? [Listen to answer] OK, so it was replaced in [year]. With most asphalt roofs, you're looking at about 20-year lifespan, so you've probably got [X years] left on this one. That's good - it means your roof will last through most of the solar system's lifespan.
>
> If your roof was getting toward the end of its life, we'd recommend addressing that before solar. But you're in good shape here."

### 2. Roof Condition: Visual Assessment

During your site survey or initial visit, visually assess the roof condition:

**Green Flags (Roof is healthy):**
- Shingles look relatively uniform in color
- No visible curling or cracking
- No missing shingles or large gaps
- Flashing appears intact around pipes and vents
- No visible sagging or structural issues

**Yellow Flags (Roof is aging but acceptable):**
- Some discoloration or weathering
- Minor cracking or curling visible
- Few missing shingles (1-3)
- Flashing shows some age but isn't leaking
- Moss or algae growth (cosmetic, not structural)

**Red Flags (Roof needs attention):**
- Multiple missing or damaged shingles
- Significant curling and cracking
- Visible damage, tears, or holes
- Flashing issues (visible damage or leaks inside)
- Sagging sections
- Visible rot or water damage from inside attic

**Your Script for Green Flags:**
> "Your roof is in excellent condition. I can see you've maintained it well. We can install solar directly on this roof without any concerns."

**Your Script for Yellow Flags:**
> "Your roof is in decent shape overall, but I can see it's aging. We can definitely install solar on it, and your roof will probably last another 7-10 years. If you want, we can recommend a roofer to evaluate it before installation, but it's not a deal-breaker."

**Your Script for Red Flags:**
> "I'm noticing your roof is showing some wear - there are a few damaged areas and some curling shingles. Before we install solar, I'd recommend getting a roof inspection. We can have our roofing partner take a look, and if it needs work, we can address that as part of the project. It's better to take care of it now than have issues later."

### 3. Roof Material: Know What You're Working With

Different roofing materials support solar differently and have different lifespans:

**Asphalt Shingles (Most Common):**
- Lifespan: 15-25 years
- Solar installation: Standard roof penetrations with flashing
- Cost to replace: $5,000-$12,000 for typical home
- Maintenance: Relatively easy
- Your approach: Standard installation, monitor for issues

**Composition Shingles:**
- Similar to asphalt shingles
- Slightly more durable (20-30 years)
- Installation and approach: Same as asphalt

**Metal Roofing:**
- Lifespan: 40-70 years
- Solar installation: Can install, but special fastening required
- Cost to replace: $12,000-$20,000+ (but rarely needs replacing)
- Maintenance: Minimal
- Your approach: This is excellent - they likely won't need roof replacement for 40+ years

**Wood Shakes/Shingles:**
- Lifespan: 20-40 years (if well-maintained)
- Solar installation: Possible, but challenging
- Cost to replace: $10,000-$15,000
- Maintenance: High
- Your approach: Doable, but assess condition carefully

**Spanish/Clay Tiles:**
- Lifespan: 50-100+ years
- Solar installation: Challenging - need special framing to distribute weight
- Cost to replace: $15,000-$25,000
- Maintenance: Relatively low
- Your approach: Possible, but requires specialized installation and framing

**Tar & Gravel Flat Roof:**
- Lifespan: 15-20 years
- Solar installation: Possible with proper engineering for weight distribution
- Cost to replace: $5,000-$10,000
- Maintenance: Regular
- Your approach: Doable but requires engineer review for weight distribution

**Your Script:**
> "Your roof is [material], which is great for solar. [If long-lasting material] Because metal roofs last 50+ years, you won't be replacing this roof during the solar system's lifespan. You're set for life.
>
> [If standard material] With asphalt shingles, they typically last 20 years. Your roof should easily last through the solar system's lifespan."

### 4. Structural Assessment: Can It Support Solar Weight?

Solar panels weigh about 40-45 pounds each. An 8kW system (24 panels) weighs 1,000+ pounds. You need to ensure the roof structure can support it.

**You as a closer:** You're not a structural engineer, but you should know the basic concerns:

**Structural considerations:**
- Rafter spacing and size
- Roof decking condition
- Existing roof load (existing systems, antennas, etc.)
- Snow load requirements (San Diego = minimal, but still code)
- Wind requirements (San Diego coastal = significant)

**Your Role:**
- Do a visual walk-around
- Look for obvious structural issues
- Note any concerns
- Trust your engineer to do the detailed assessment
- Use structural assessment as a value-add: "Our engineer will do a full structural review to ensure your roof can handle the system safely"

**Your Script:**
> "Our engineers will do a complete structural assessment to ensure your roof can safely support the solar system. They'll look at the rafters, the decking, and the overall integrity. It's one of the reasons we do an on-site survey - we want to make sure everything is engineered perfectly for your specific home."
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="The Re-Roofing Conversation">
This is the challenging scenario, but it's an opportunity:

### Scenario 1: Roof is too old to proceed without replacement

**Your Situation:**
- Customer's roof is 22+ years old
- You know they'll need replacement during solar system lifespan
- Or roof has damage that needs immediate attention

**Your Approach:**

Step 1 - Be transparent and helpful:
> "Jimmy, I want to be straight with you. Your roof is 22 years old, which puts it near the end of its lifespan. We could install solar on it today, but I think you'd face a roof replacement in the next 5-7 years. That means pulling off the solar panels, replacing the roof, and reinstalling the panels. That's expensive and a hassle.
>
> Here's what I recommend: Let's coordinate a roof replacement now, before solar. It actually makes the project simpler and saves you money overall."

Step 2 - Position it as a package:
> "Here's what happens: We'll have our roofing partner give you a quote on the roof replacement. Once that's done, we install the solar on the new roof. Your new roof has a 25-year warranty, and your solar has a 25-year warranty. Everything is new and protected for 25 years. It's the smart move."

Step 3 - Break down the real cost:
> "Yes, it costs more upfront. But you're replacing the roof anyway - it's not like solar is causing it. The difference is doing it with solar planned in, versus doing it separately and then dealing with solar later.
>
> When you do roof + solar at the same time, you get contractor discounts, and you avoid the expense of pulling off and reinstalling panels later. You're actually saving money by doing it together."

**The Pitch:**
> "I'm not here to pressure you. This is your decision. But if it were my home, I'd replace the roof now and install solar on the new roof. Start fresh, everything protected, zero worries for 25 years."

### Scenario 2: Roof damage that needs repair

**Your Situation:**
- Roof has some damage but isn't at end-of-life
- Damage is localized (a few damaged shingles, flashing issue)
- Can be repaired without full replacement

**Your Approach:**

Step 1 - Identify and quantify:
> "I noticed a few damaged shingles and the flashing around this vent needs attention. Those are small fixes, nothing major."

Step 2 - Offer to connect them with roofer:
> "I can have our roofing partner come out and give you a quote. It should be a few hundred bucks to patch those areas. Once that's done, you're set for solar."

Step 3 - Explain the timeline:
> "We'll coordinate the timing - roof repair first, then solar installation. The repairs take a day, then we're ready to install your system. No delays on the solar side."

**The Pitch:**
> "This is a minor fix. It's better to address it now while we've got a roof contractor already on the job. Then you're all set."
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Red Flags That Require Engineer Review">
As a closer, if you see any of these, flag it for your engineering team before proceeding:

1. **Obvious structural damage** - Sagging roof sections, visible rot
2. **Active water damage** - Stains inside the attic or interior
3. **Problematic roof geometry** - Multiple levels, dormers, or steep angles that complicate installation
4. **Metal roofing with concerns** - Rust, deterioration, or poor connection points
5. **Flat roof with poor drainage** - Pooling water or past water damage
6. **Significant moss/algae** - Suggests moisture retention and potential deterioration
7. **Bird or wildlife damage** - Holes or openings in the roof
8. **Asbestos concerns** - Older homes with certain roofing materials (rare, but critical)

**Your Script for Red Flags:**
> "I want our engineer to take a closer look at this before we commit. I'm flagging this for our team, and they'll do a full assessment before we move forward. It might be fine, or there might be some special considerations. Either way, we'll make sure it's safe and sound before we install anything."
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Roof Assessment Best Practices">
### What to Look For (Checklist)

When you're at the property doing your initial assessment:

**Visual Inspection Checklist:**
- [ ] Overall roof condition (green/yellow/red flag)
- [ ] Age of roof (when was it last replaced?)
- [ ] Material type (asphalt, metal, tile, flat, etc.)
- [ ] Any visible damage (missing shingles, tears, holes)
- [ ] Flashing condition (pipes, vents, chimney)
- [ ] Gutters and drainage (clean, functional, any damage?)
- [ ] Nearby trees (overhanging branches, leaves, debris)
- [ ] Roof pitch/angle (too steep affects installation approach)
- [ ] Multiple roof levels or dormers (affects layout)
- [ ] Any other rooftop equipment (antennas, HVAC, existing utilities)

### How to Talk About It

**For excellent roofs:**
> "Your roof is in fantastic condition. We can install your solar system without any concerns, and your roof will easily last through the entire 25-year warranty period. Perfect situation."

**For good roofs:**
> "Your roof is in good shape overall. It should have plenty of life left in it - probably 10+ years. When the time comes to replace it down the road, you'll have a beautiful solar-friendly roof to install the new system on."

**For aging roofs that still work:**
> "Your roof is getting to the end of its lifespan, but it's still functional. We can install solar on it. You'll probably replace it sometime in the next 5-10 years, and when you do, we'll coordinate with our roofing partner to make it seamless."

**For roofs that need attention:**
> "I'm recommending we address the roof before installing solar. This isn't a problem - it's actually great because we can bundle everything together. New roof, new solar, everything protected for 25 years."
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Key Takeaways">
1. **Assess roof age first** - It's the biggest factor in planning
2. **Know the visual red flags** - You don't need to be an expert, just observant
3. **Understand the material** - Different roofs require different approaches
4. **Re-roofing is an opportunity, not a problem** - Bundle it with solar for better value
5. **Flag concerns for your engineer** - That's what they're there for
6. **Use roof assessment as proof of thoroughness** - It builds confidence
