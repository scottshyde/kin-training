# Griffin Hill Closing System

As a closer, you own routines 3 through 6 of the Griffin Hill system: Solution Presentation, Adapted Solution, Closing Interactions, and Fulfillment & Follow Up. This is where deals are won or lost. Every play in this section assumes you have already completed a thorough Needs Audit — if you skipped it or rushed it, go back and finish before presenting.

<Accordion title="Solution Presentation (SP) — Propose, Persuade, Convince">
The Solution Presentation is not a product demo. It is a personalized recommendation built entirely on what the homeowner told you during the Needs Audit. If your SP does not reference their words, their numbers, and their pain, it is a generic pitch and it will feel like one.

### Play 1: Reframe (CO + NA)

Before you present anything, summarize everything you have learned. This proves you listened, confirms you understand their situation, and creates the bridge to your proposal.

> "So let me make sure I've got this right. You've been in the home for about 8 years. Your summer bills are running around $350, and you've seen them go up every year. You mentioned you're frustrated because you feel like there's nothing you can do about it — SDG&E just keeps raising rates. And you said if you could cut that bill in half, you'd put that money toward your kids' college fund. Did I get that right?"

Wait for them to confirm. If they correct you, update your understanding. This is not a formality — it is the foundation of your entire presentation.

### Play 2: Transition via Benefit

Bridge from their situation to your solution using the benefit that matters most to them. You identified this during the NA — now use it.

> "Well, here's the good news — based on everything you just told me, this is exactly the kind of situation where we can make a big difference. Let me show you what this looks like for your specific home."

### Play 3: Proposal — The FABP Method

Present your solution using Feature, Advantage, Benefit, Proof for each key element. Do not list features. Connect every feature to something they said they care about.

| Element | What It Is | Example |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| **Feature** | What the product/service includes | "Your system is a 9.6 kW array with 24 panels" |
| **Advantage** | Why that feature matters generally | "That's sized to offset 100% of your annual usage" |
| **Benefit** | Why it matters to them specifically | "Which means that $350 summer bill goes to about $15 — the grid connection fee" |
| **Proof** | Evidence it works | "Here's the Rodriguez family two streets over — same size home, same usage. Here's their bill before and after" |

**Full FABP example at the table:**
> "So your system is a 9.6 kW array — 24 panels on your south-facing roof. The advantage is that it's sized to offset 100% of your annual electricity usage. What that means for you is that $350 summer bill drops to about $15, which is just the grid connection fee. And this isn't hypothetical — here's the Rodriguez family on Elm Street. Same size home, similar usage. Here's their bill from last July: $12.47."

### Statement of Work & Mutual Expectations

Clearly outline what KIN Home will do and what the homeowner can expect. Transparency builds trust.

> "Here's exactly how this works. We handle everything — permitting, engineering, install, inspections, and getting you connected to NEM. Your job is to be available for the install day, which is typically 4-6 weeks out, and to keep your roof clear. After that, the system runs itself."

### Play 4: Service Levels & Pricing

Present pricing with confidence. Do not apologize for the cost — tie it back to the savings they have already agreed they want.

> "So there are two ways to go. Option one is a PPA at [rate] per kilowatt-hour — that's about 20-30% less than what SDG&E is charging you today, and your rate locks in. No upfront cost, nothing out of pocket. Option two is a purchase, which gives you the full ownership benefits and tax credit. Based on what you told me about wanting to save long-term, most homeowners in your situation go with [recommendation]. Here's why..."

### Play 5: Call to Action Close

Ask for the commitment directly. Do not hint. Do not trail off. Make it clear and simple.

> "Based on everything we've gone over, I think this is a no-brainer for your family. Let's get you locked in tonight so we can start the permitting process and get you saving as soon as possible. I just need your signature right here to get the ball rolling."

If they say yes, proceed to signing. If they hesitate, move to the additional plays below.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="The Insurance Play">
Use this when a homeowner is interested but nervous about making a mistake. The Insurance Play reduces perceived risk by highlighting protections and safety nets.

> "I totally get it — this is a big decision and you want to make sure you're protected. Here's what I want you to know. First, there's a 3-day right of rescission — meaning if you sign tonight and wake up tomorrow morning with second thoughts, you can cancel with zero consequences. Second, your system comes with a 25-year warranty. Third, KIN Home has been doing this for [X] years and we're not going anywhere. You're not jumping off a cliff here — you've got a parachute, a backup parachute, and a safety net at the bottom."

**When to use it:** The homeowner says things like "I just want to make sure," "This is a big commitment," or "What if something goes wrong?"

**Key principle:** You are not overcoming an objection — you are validating their concern and showing them they are already protected.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="The Validation Play">
Use this when a homeowner raises a concern that is actually reasonable. Instead of arguing, validate it — then redirect.

> "You know what? That's a really smart question, and honestly, I'd be asking the same thing if I were in your shoes. The fact that you're thinking this through tells me you're the kind of person who makes good decisions. And here's what I've found — the homeowners who ask the toughest questions up front are the happiest customers six months later, because they went in with eyes wide open."

**When to use it:** The homeowner raises a legitimate concern — roof condition, rate changes, contract terms, installation timeline. Things that deserve a real answer.

**Follow up with:** The actual answer to their question. Validation without substance is empty. Validate, then inform.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="The Ignore Play">
Some objections are not real objections. They are reflexive responses, stall tactics, or conversational filler. The Ignore Play is knowing when to not engage.

**Examples of ignorable statements:**
- "We need to think about it" (said immediately, before they have actually processed anything)
- "We never make decisions the same day" (often untrue — they bought their car, their house, their TV on the spot)
- "Let me do some research" (usually means they want you to leave)

**How to execute:**
Acknowledge briefly, then redirect to a question or a new piece of value. Do not argue with the statement or try to overcome it directly.

> **Homeowner:** "We never make same-day decisions."
> **You:** "I totally respect that. Let me ask you this — setting aside the timing for a second, does the savings itself make sense to you? Is this something you'd want to do if the timing was right?"

If they say yes, the objection was not about the product — it was about comfort. Continue building comfort. If they say no, you have a different problem to diagnose.

**Key principle:** Not every objection deserves a full response. Some just need a redirect. The skill is knowing the difference.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Overcoming Objections — Identify, Isolate, Evaluate">
When a homeowner raises a real objection, use the three-step process: Identify, Isolate, Evaluate.

### Step 1: Identify

Find out what the objection actually is. Most homeowners give you the surface version, not the real version.

> **Homeowner:** "I need to think about it."
> **You:** "Absolutely, I want you to feel 100% confident. Help me understand — when you say you need to think about it, what specifically are you thinking through? Is it the savings, the timing, or something else?"

### Step 2: Isolate

Make sure this is the only concern. If you solve this one and another pops up, you are chasing a moving target.

> **You:** "Okay, so the main concern is whether your roof will need to be replaced in the next few years. If we could address that — if you knew the roof was good — would you be ready to move forward tonight?"

If they say yes, you have isolated the objection. If they say "well, also..." then you have more work to do. Keep isolating until you have the real, single concern.

### Step 3: Evaluate — Simple vs. Complex

**Simple objections** are based on misunderstanding or missing information. Handle them with Feel/Felt/Found:

> **You:** "I totally understand how you feel. A lot of homeowners I've worked with felt the same way — they were worried about the roof too. What they found was that we actually do a full roof assessment before we install, and if there's any issue, we address it as part of the project. In fact, the Garcias on Maple Street had the same concern, and it turned out their roof was in great shape for another 15 years."

**The Feel/Felt/Found pattern:**
1. **Feel** — Validate their emotion. "I understand how you feel"
2. **Felt** — Normalize it. "Other homeowners have felt the same way"
3. **Found** — Resolve it with evidence. "What they found was..."

**Complex objections** are based on real constraints — financial hardship, genuine roof issues, pending home sale, credit problems. These require deeper discovery, not a word track. Ask more questions. Understand the full picture. Sometimes the answer is "this isn't the right time" — and saying that with integrity earns you a referral later.

### Common Objections at the KIN Home Table

| Objection | Type | Response Approach |
|-----------|------|------------------|
| "I need to think about it" | Often ignorable / simple | Identify what specifically. Isolate. Then Feel/Felt/Found or Ignore Play |
| "My spouse isn't here" | Process failure (SNE) | "I understand. When will they be home? Let's schedule 30 minutes so I can walk you both through it together" |
| "Solar doesn't work / I've heard bad things" | Simple (misinformation) | Feel/Felt/Found with local proof. Show neighbor results |
| "I can't afford it" | Could be simple or complex | Isolate: is it cash flow or credit? PPA has zero upfront cost. If credit, that is a real constraint |
| "I'm selling my home" | Complex (real constraint) | Solar increases home value by 4-6%. If selling within 6 months, may not be right time. Be honest |
| "I already have solar" | Real constraint | "Great! How's your experience been? Are you happy with your rate?" Explore if there's an upgrade opportunity |
| "Rates might go down" | Simple (misinformation) | "In the last 20 years, SDG&E rates have gone up every single year. Here's the chart" |
| "I want to get other quotes" | Can be stall or legitimate | "Absolutely, you should compare. What are you going to compare? Let me make sure you know what to look for so you don't get taken advantage of" |
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Adapted Solution (AS) — When the First Proposal Doesn't Close">
If the homeowner does not close on your initial Solution Presentation, do not give up and do not repeat yourself. The Adapted Solution routine is a strategic reset.

### The AS Process

**Step 1: Reframe CO + NA + SP**

Summarize everything again, but this time incorporate what you learned from their objection. Their "no" gave you new information. Use it.

> "Okay, so let me step back. You told me your bill is $350, you want to save money for your kids' college fund, and the savings make sense to you. The concern is that you're not comfortable with a 25-year agreement because you might move in 5-7 years. Do I have that right?"

**Step 2: Adapt the Proposal**

Change the solution to address their specific concern. Different financing option, different system size, different framing.

> "Here's what I'd recommend instead. Let's look at the PPA option — there's no upfront cost and if you sell the home, the agreement transfers to the next buyer. In fact, homes with solar sell faster and for about 4% more. So instead of a liability, this actually becomes a selling point. And in the meantime, you're saving $150/month. Let me show you how this looks."

**Step 3: Call to Action Close (Again)**

> "Does this version make more sense for your situation? Great — let's get this locked in so you start saving right away."

**Key principle:** The AS is not begging. It is problem-solving. You got new data from their objection, and you are using it to build a better solution. If you cannot adapt because their concern is legitimate and unsolvable, say so honestly.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Closing Interactions (CI) — The Dangerous Delay Period">
When a homeowner does not close on the spot and you cannot adapt to a close, you enter the most dangerous phase of the sale: the delay period. Every day that passes, the probability of closing drops. Closing Interactions keep the deal alive.

### The Give/Get Framework

Every CI touchpoint must include both:

**Give something** — A new piece of value they did not have before.
- A rate increase announcement: "SDG&E just announced another 6% increase starting January. Wanted you to see this"
- A neighbor update: "Hey, the Johnsons two doors down just got their system turned on. They're already seeing the savings"
- A deadline: "The current incentive rate expires at the end of the month — wanted to make sure you didn't miss it"
- An answer: Follow up on something they asked that you needed to look into

**Get a mini-commitment** — Something that moves them one step closer.
- "Can you pull up your most recent SDG&E bill and send me a photo?"
- "Have you had a chance to talk to your wife about it?"
- "I'm going to be in the neighborhood Thursday — can I stop by for 10 minutes to show you the updated proposal?"

### Schedule Next Event — Always

Never end a CI without a specific next event. "I'll follow up next week" is not a next event. "I'll call you Thursday at 6pm" is.

> "I'll give you a call Thursday at 6 after you've had a chance to talk to Maria. If I don't hear from you, I'll shoot you a text Friday morning. Sound good?"

### CI Cadence for Pending Deals

| Days Since Sit | Action |
|----------------|--------|
| Day 1 | Text: "Great meeting you and [spouse]. Let me know if any questions come up" |
| Day 3 | Call: Give new value + get mini-commitment |
| Day 5 | Text or drop-by: Give proof (neighbor result, review) |
| Day 7 | Call: Direct ask — "Have you made a decision?" + new Call to Action |
| Day 10+ | If no response, shift to monthly check-in. Do not burn the bridge |

**Key principle:** The difference between following up and being annoying is value. If every touchpoint gives them something useful, you are being helpful. If every touchpoint is "just checking in," you are being a pest.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Fulfillment & Follow Up (FF) — Close the Loop">
The sale is not over when they sign. FF is where you build your reputation, your referral pipeline, and your proof library.

### Fulfill Promises

Everything you said during the sale must happen. If you said "4-6 weeks to install," keep them updated on progress. If you said "we handle everything," do not make them chase down permitting updates. A fulfilled promise turns a customer into an advocate. A broken promise turns them into a cancellation or a bad review.

**Post-sign communication cadence:**
- Day 1: Welcome text/call confirming next steps
- Weekly: Status update on permitting/engineering
- Install day: Be present or send a congratulations message
- Day 1 post-install: "How does it look? Any questions?"

### Collect Proofs

Proofs are the fuel for your setters' doors and your own future presentations. Collect them proactively — do not wait for customers to volunteer.

**What to collect:**
- Google review (send them the direct link — make it easy)
- Before/after bill screenshot
- Video testimonial (even 30 seconds on their porch is gold)
- Photo of the install on their roof

**When to ask:** After the system is activated and they have seen their first low bill. That is the peak of excitement.

> "Hey [Name], I saw your first bill came in at $14 — that's awesome! Would you mind leaving us a quick Google review? Here's the link. It really helps other families in the neighborhood find us. And if you're up for it, I'd love to grab a quick 30-second video of you talking about the experience."

### Harvest Referrals

A happy customer knows other people with high electric bills. Ask them.

> "By the way, who else do you know in the neighborhood who's been complaining about their SDG&E bill? I'd love to help them out the same way."

**Referral timing:** Ask after fulfillment, not before. They need to experience the result before they will stake their reputation on recommending you.

### Schedule Next Event

Check in at 30, 60, and 90 days post-install. After that, quarterly or when rate changes are announced. These check-ins keep the referral door open and catch any issues before they become complaints.

> "I'm going to check in with you in about a month to see how the savings are looking. I'll call you on [date]. And if anything comes up before then, you've got my cell."
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="The Call-to-Action Close Play — Pattern and Examples">
The Call-to-Action Close (CTA Close) is not a single moment — it is a pattern you can use multiple times throughout the SP and AS routines. The pattern has three parts:

### The Pattern

1. **Summarize the benefit** (what they get)
2. **Create urgency** (why now)
3. **Direct ask** (what to do next)

### Example 1: First CTA Close (end of SP)
> "So you're looking at saving $150 a month — that's $1,800 a year back in your pocket for your family. Right now, you're locked in at today's rate before the next SDG&E increase hits. Let's get the paperwork started so we can get you on the install calendar."

### Example 2: CTA Close After Objection Handling
> "So now that we've addressed the roof question and you know you're covered, you're saving $150 a month with zero upfront cost and a full warranty. Let's get you signed up tonight so you don't lose this rate."

### Example 3: CTA Close in Adapted Solution
> "With the PPA option, there's nothing out of pocket, you save from day one, and if you sell the house it transfers to the buyer. This checks every box you mentioned. I just need your signature right here to kick things off."

### Example 4: CTA Close in Closing Interaction (Follow-Up)
> "Hey [Name], SDG&E just announced another rate increase for January. You're going to be paying even more than that $350 if we don't lock you in now. I have an opening on the install calendar for next month. Can we get you signed up this week?"

**Key principle:** Every CTA Close ties back to their words and their pain. If you find yourself making a generic pitch, you did not do a good enough Needs Audit. Go back to what they told you and use it.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Closer Mindset: Integrity in the Close">
Griffin Hill is an integrity system. That means:

- **Never pressure someone into a deal that is wrong for them.** If solar does not make sense for their situation, say so. You will earn their respect and their referrals
- **Always tell the truth about savings projections.** Overpromising creates cancellations, bad reviews, and a reputation that kills your pipeline
- **Follow through on every promise.** The easiest way to build a referral business is to do what you said you would do
- **Treat every homeowner like your neighbor.** Because in many cases, they are. Your setters will be back on that street tomorrow
- **Close with confidence, not desperation.** You are offering them a solution to a real problem. If the solution fits, ask for the commitment. If it does not fit, have the integrity to walk away

The best closers do not need tricks. They need a thorough Needs Audit, a personalized proposal, and the confidence to ask for the business. The system gives you all three — execute it.
</Accordion>
