# Overcoming Objections in Close

## The Foundation: A Great Needs Audit

The best overcoming objection play-set is an **exceptional needs audit**. If you nail the needs audit, you may never even have to overcome an objection. The objection won't surface in the first place.

> **Key Principle:** The difference between a good closer and an exceptional one is whether they get the business the first time around or have to come back multiple times. A quality needs audit is your secret weapon.

## The Three Types of Objections

When a customer stalls or expresses concern, it will almost always fall into one of three categories:

1. **Time** - They want to think about it, need more time to decide, want to wait
2. **Money** - They have financial concerns, worry about the investment, or think it's too expensive
3. **People** - The decision maker isn't present, spouse isn't convinced, they need to consult someone else

Understanding which category the objection falls into helps you respond with the right play-set.

## Play-Set 1: The Identification Process

Use this play-set to fully understand and clarify the objection before jumping to solutions.

### Step 1: Identify
- **Existence**: Confirm the objection exists - "So cost is a concern for you?"
- **Nature**: Understand what specifically they're concerned about - "Tell me more about that"
- **Restate**: Confirm your understanding - "So you're concerned that the monthly payment might be higher than you expected?"

### Step 2: Isolate
- **Other Concerns**: "Are there any other concerns besides that?" (Keep asking until they say no)
- **Biggest Barrier**: "Of all the concerns you've mentioned, which one would be the biggest barrier to moving forward?"

### Step 3: Evaluate
- **Simple or Complex**: Determine if this is straightforward to overcome or will require deep work
- **Qualification Check**: Does this objection relate back to something in your needs audit? Did you miss something?

## Play-Set 2: The Solution Process

Once you've identified the objection, use this systematic approach to overcome it.

### Step 1: Empathize
**Feel, Felt, Found** - Make them feel heard and understood:
- "I know exactly how you **feel**"
- "In fact, the Daniels next door **felt** the same way"
- "What we've **found** at KOTA is [solution]"

### Step 2: Solve
- **Minimize the barrier** - Show how the concern is smaller than they think
- **Provide context** - Use comparison, proof, or logic to reframe the issue

### Step 3: Benefit
- Connect back to their stated needs from the needs audit
- Show how overcoming this objection unlocks the benefits they said they wanted

### Step 4: Proof
- Use a proof play - neighbor success story, data, or similar situation
- "Jimmy is on his way to save $60,000 over the next 25 years. I know I can do the same for you."

### Step 5: Relate
- Connect the overcoming objection back to their original concerns
- Show how this solution addresses what matters to them

### Step 6: CTAC (Call to Action Close)
- "That being said, let's go ahead and get you qualified"
- "Who's name would you prefer to put this in?"

## Master Word Track for Overcoming Objections

Use this proven word track as your framework, adapting it to the specific objection:

> "It makes perfect sense to me that you want to make the best decision. Based on our conversation, what are you not 100% on today? Can you help me better understand [Open Ended Question, Drill Down]?
>
> I know exactly how you feel. In fact, the Daniels next door felt the same way. What we've found at KOTA is when we cut corners we diminish the customer experience. We give the best equipment, the best warranties, at a fair price.
>
> Jimmy is on his way to save $60,000 over the next 25 years. I know I can do the same for you. That being said, let's go ahead and get you qualified. Who's name would you prefer to put this in?"

## Power Drill-Down Questions

When you hear an objection like "I want to think about it," use these questions to understand the real barrier:

### For "I want to think about it":
- "Help me understand what would be your **perceived risk** with moving forward?"
- "What **boxes need to be checked** with us for you to feel comfortable to roll forward?"
- "What **else would you need to know** before having this become a reality for you?"

### For "It's too expensive":
- "What would be a comfortable monthly investment for you?"
- "Is it the total price or the monthly payment that's the concern?"
- "Walk me through your biggest expense right now - the solar or the SDGE bill?"

### For "I need to talk to my spouse/family":
- "When are they available so we can all talk through this together?"
- "What do you think would be their main question or concern?"
- "If your spouse were here right now, what would you want them to understand first?"

## The Philosophy Behind Drill-Down

Remember this powerful principle:

> "Being more educated on anything will always lead to confident decision-making."

When customers feel like they don't know enough, they hesitate. Your job is to educate them until they have the confidence to make a decision. The more informed they are about:
- How solar works
- How their savings work
- What the warranty covers
- The timeline of the project

...the more likely they are to move forward without objections.

## Call to Action Close - Two Approaches

Once you've overcome the objection, you need a clear call to action:

### Approach 1: Qualification
> "The next step is to get you qualified. I just need to verify some basic information from you."

### Approach 2: Site Survey
> "Let's schedule your site survey. That being said, let's go ahead and get you qualified."

Both approaches move the conversation from objection-handling to action-taking. You're not asking permission to move forward - you're confirming the next logical step.

## Common Objections by Category

### Time-Based Objections
- "I want to think about it"
- "Let me talk to my spouse/accountant"
- "Can we do this next quarter?"
- "I'm not ready yet"

**Response Strategy**: Ask drill-down questions to understand if it's truly a timing issue or if there's a hidden concern. Often "I need time" really means "I don't feel confident enough yet."

### Money-Based Objections
- "It's too expensive"
- "The payment is higher than I expected"
- "I'm not getting enough savings"
- "What if my roof needs replacing?"

**Response Strategy**: Go back to their needs audit. What savings number did they want? Reframe the investment in terms of their current SDGE bill. Show the 25-year picture.

### People-Based Objections
- "My spouse needs to be here"
- "I need to talk to my accountant"
- "This is a big decision for our family"
- "My business partner won't agree to this"

**Response Strategy**: Respect the decision-making process. Offer to present together. Get buy-in from the primary decision-maker in the room, then schedule a time to present to the other stakeholders.

## The Objection Prevention Mindset

The truth is, you overcome most objections **before they become objections**. How?

1. **Deep Discovery**: Ask thorough needs audit questions until you understand their budget, timeline, and decision-making process
2. **Clear Communication**: Present solutions that directly address the concerns they shared
3. **Transparency**: Address potential concerns before they bring them up
4. **Social Proof**: Share success stories of people similar to them who overcame the same initial hesitations

## Key Takeaways

- Objections are a sign you're getting close to a decision
- Never skip the identification process - you have to understand the real objection first
- The Feel-Felt-Found pattern creates rapport while you solve their concern
- Always connect back to their original needs from the appointment
- End with a clear call to action, not a question
- The best objection handling is prevention through an excellent needs audit
