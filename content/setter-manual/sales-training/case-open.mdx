# The Case Open

The first 60 words carry greater weight and importance than the next 6,000. Everything you do at the door lives or dies in those first seconds. The purpose of the Case Open is to engineer a **yes** at your Ask for Permission.

> **If you're talking to 8-10 decision makers and getting 8-10 Case Open wins, you're delivering with absolute sauce. If you're not hitting those numbers, the work is on your non-verbal and paraverbal communication — not the script.**

<Accordion title="The Rule You'll Break">
Stay disciplined to the script. You will be tempted to muddy the water with your own sauce. When you use more than 60 words, you give your prospect a lower probability of retaining the message you want understood.

Stay to 60 words. You won't. But try.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="The Word Track">
Each component has a word count. Hit all five. Stay under 60.

| Component | Script | Words |
|-----------|--------|-------|
| **Rapport** | Hey, how are you? I'm with Kin Home. I helped your neighbor Sean... | 12 |
| **Position** | ...qualify for the new virtual utility plant... | 7 |
| **Benefit** | ...for $0 money down. | 4 |
| **Proof** | Sean paid $141 with SDGE, I replaced that, now he pays just $99 bucks for battery and solar. | 20 |
| **Ask for Permission** | This is completely different than the other thousand guys, do you mind if I ask you just 2 questions? | 18 |
| | **Total** | **61** |
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Breaking Down Each Component">
### Rapport
This is not a greeting. It's an anchor. You mention your company and immediately attach it to a real neighbor. "I helped your neighbor Sean" establishes social proof before the prospect has had time to form an objection.

### Position Statement
"Qualify for the new virtual utility plant" does two things: it's unfamiliar (not "solar"), and it implies scarcity. They have to qualify. Not everyone gets in.

### Benefit
Four words. "$0 money down." Don't add to it. The brevity is the point.

### Proof
Real numbers from a real neighbor. Not "we save people money." Sean paid $141. Sean now pays $99. Tangible. Local. Verifiable.

### Ask for Permission
The phrase "completely different" does the heavy lifting here. You're acknowledging the noise in their head — *I've had 10 solar guys knock on this door* — and disarming it in four words before asking for two questions.

Once they say yes, the dynamic has shifted. You asked permission. They granted it. Now they're listening.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="The Adapted Case Open (When They Already Know It's Solar)">
If the prospect sniffs it out early — *"Is this solar?"* — don't panic. Pivot:

> "You've probably had a lot of guys coming by to sell you solar... those guys make my job difficult. Do you mind if I ask you just two questions?"

This accomplishes the same goal. You validate their experience, separate yourself from the noise, and get to the Ask for Permission. See the **Smokescreens** article for the full "Is this solar?" response set.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Common Mistakes">
**Talking too fast.** Nervousness drives speed. Slow down. A confident rep doesn't rush. The pause after "I helped your neighbor Sean" is intentional — let it breathe.

**Adding your own words.** Every extra sentence you add is another chance for them to find an exit. The script is 60 words for a reason.

**Weak energy on the Ask for Permission.** This is not a question you whisper. Deliver it with a slight smile and genuine curiosity. You're not begging for a moment. You're offering them something their neighbors already have.

**Skipping the Proof.** The real neighbor with real numbers is the single most important element of the Case Open. If you make up a generic neighbor, prospects can feel it. Use actual installs in the area.
</Accordion>
