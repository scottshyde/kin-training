# The Case Open

<Lead>The first 60 words carry greater weight and importance than the next 6,000. Everything you do at the door lives or dies in those first seconds. The purpose of the Case Open is to engineer a **yes** at your Ask for Permission.</Lead>

<Callout type="important" title="THE STANDARD">
If you're talking to 8-10 decision makers and getting 8-10 Case Open wins, you're delivering with absolute sauce. If you're not hitting those numbers, the work is on your **non-verbal** and **paraverbal** communication — not the script.
</Callout>

<GoldRule />

<Accordion title="The Rule You'll Break">

<Callout type="warning" title="DISCIPLINE">
Stay to the script. You will be tempted to muddy the water with your own sauce.
</Callout>

When you use more than 60 words, you give your prospect a **lower probability** of retaining the message you want understood.

<PullQuote>Stay to 60 words. You won't. But try.</PullQuote>

</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Breaking Down Each Component">

### Rapport

This is not a greeting. It's an **anchor**. You mention your company and immediately attach it to a real neighbor.

<KeyTerm term="Social Proof Anchor">"I helped your neighbor Sean" establishes social proof before the prospect has had time to form an objection.</KeyTerm>

### Position Statement

<PullQuote>Qualify for the new virtual utility plant.</PullQuote>

<Callout type="insight">
"Virtual utility plant" does two things: it's **unfamiliar** (not "solar"), and it implies **scarcity**. They have to qualify. Not everyone gets in.
</Callout>

### Benefit

Four words. **"$0 money down."** Don't add to it. The brevity is the point.

### Proof

Real numbers from a real neighbor. Not "we save people money." Sean paid **$141**. Sean now pays **$99**. Tangible. Local. Verifiable.

<Callout type="tip">
Use actual install numbers from the neighborhood you're in. Made-up numbers feel made-up. Real numbers land.
</Callout>

### Ask for Permission

The phrase **"completely different"** does the heavy lifting. You're acknowledging the noise in their head — *I've had 10 solar guys knock on this door* — and disarming it in four words before asking for two questions.

<KeyTerm term="Permission Shift">Once they say yes, the dynamic has shifted. You asked permission. They granted it. Now they're listening.</KeyTerm>

</Accordion>

<GoldRule />

<Accordion title="The Adapted Case Open">

<Callout type="warning" title="WHEN THEY ALREADY KNOW IT'S SOLAR">
If the prospect sniffs it out early — "Is this solar?" — don't panic. Pivot.
</Callout>

<PullQuote>You've probably had a lot of guys coming by to sell you solar... those guys make my job difficult. Do you mind if I ask you just two questions?</PullQuote>

This accomplishes the same goal. You **validate** their experience, **separate** yourself from the noise, and get to the Ask for Permission. See the **Smokescreens** article for the full "Is this solar?" response set.

</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Common Mistakes">

<Steps>
<Step number={1} title="Talking Too Fast">
Nervousness drives speed. **Slow down.** A confident rep doesn't rush. The pause after "I helped your neighbor Sean" is intentional — let it breathe.
</Step>

<Step number={2} title="Adding Your Own Words">
Every extra sentence you add is another chance for them to find an exit. The script is 60 words for a reason.
</Step>

<Step number={3} title="Weak Energy on the AFP">
This is not a question you whisper. Deliver it with a slight smile and genuine curiosity. You're not begging for a moment. You're offering them something their neighbors already have.
</Step>

<Step number={4} title="Skipping the Proof">
The real neighbor with real numbers is the **single most important element** of the Case Open. If you make up a generic neighbor, prospects can feel it. Use actual installs in the area.
</Step>
</Steps>

</Accordion>
