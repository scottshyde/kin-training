# Door Pitch 2025

The 2025 pitch runs on the same five-part framework as 2026 — Rapport, Position, Benefit, Proof, AFP. The difference is in the proof point and the SDGE education sequence. Knowing both versions makes you more adaptable in the field.

<Accordion title="The Five-Part Structure">
### Rapport
> *"Hey, how are you? I'm with KIN Home. I helped your neighbor Sean over on [street]."*

### Position
> *"We're qualifying homeowners for the new virtual utility plant."*

### Benefit
> *"For zero money down."*

### Proof
> *"Sean was paying [amount] to SDGE. I replaced that bill — now he pays [lower amount] for solar and battery backup."*

*(Use real numbers from your actual pipeline — specificity is credibility.)*

### AFP
> *"This is completely different than what you've heard before. Do you mind if I ask you just two questions?"*
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Needs Audit Bridge">
Once they agree to two questions, open with the bridge:

> *"Sean didn't do old solar for years because the price, the debt, and the savings just didn't make sense — what stopped you from looking at it?"*

This borrows the objection from "Sean" so they don't have to volunteer it themselves. It's an invitation, not a cross-examination.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="SDGE Education">
When the homeowner wants to understand why their bill is so high, walk through the three-part story:

**1. San Onofre**
San Onofre nuclear plant shut down in 2012. The region lost 2,200 megawatts of locally generated power overnight.

**2. Desert Import**
San Diego now imports electricity from the desert. That power travels hundreds of miles before it reaches a home — and someone pays for that transmission infrastructure.

**3. Delivery Charges**
Delivery charges now make up roughly 80% of a typical SDGE bill. You can conserve energy aggressively and still owe most of your bill in delivery fees alone.

> *"Most people think if they turn things off, they'll lower their bill. But 80% of what you're paying isn't for the energy — it's just to have the wire connected to your house."*
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="The Solve">
> *"What we do is replace your SDGE bill. Instead of paying the utility every month, you're paying into your own system. Solar generates the energy, the battery stores it, and you stop paying SDGE for delivery."*

Then bridge to the virtual utility plant concept: your home feeds excess energy back into the grid and you get compensated. You go from a customer of the utility to a participant in the grid.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="The SDGE Hypothetical Close">
> *"Hypothetically — if SDGE called you tonight and said, 'We'll lock in your current bill at $180 a month for the next 25 years, and at the end of 25 years we just stop charging you' — would you take that deal?"*

Almost everyone says yes.

> *"That's essentially what solar does. Except instead of paying SDGE, you're paying into something you own."*
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Takeaway and Booking">
> *"I want to be honest — not every home qualifies. Let me do a quick panel check while I'm here, grab your bill, and in about 90 seconds I'll know whether it even makes sense for you."*

The "panel check" moves you around to see the roof. The "90 seconds" language removes pressure — it's brief and contained.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Set Expectations">
Before you leave, tell them what happens at the appointment:

> *"Two things happen when my manager comes by: first, just two questions — same ones I asked. And second, if it makes sense, they'll design a system with exact rates, do a soft credit check, and walk you through paperwork if you want to move forward."*

Reps who set expectations clearly have significantly lower no-show rates. The homeowner knows what they're agreeing to.
</Accordion>
