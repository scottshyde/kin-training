# Door Pitch 2026

The door pitch is the first 60 seconds of every conversation. It's not a sales pitch — it's an engineered sequence designed to get one thing: an AFP (agreement for permission to proceed). Everything after depends on nailing this.

> **Rule:** The first 60 words carry more weight than the next 6,000.

<Accordion title="The Five-Part Structure">
Every door pitch follows the same architecture, every time. No shortcuts, no riffing.

### 1. Rapport (~12 words)
Open human. Sound like a neighbor, not a rep.

> *"Hey, how are you? I'm with KIN Home. I helped your neighbor Sean over on Maple."*

The neighbor name is your social proof before you've said anything about solar. If you don't have a name, use the cross street.

### 2. Position (~7 words)
Name what you're doing here. Be specific.

> *"We're helping homeowners qualify for the new virtual utility plant program."*

"Virtual utility plant" is intentional — it's accurate, it's different, and it creates curiosity without triggering the "solar guy" defense.

### 3. Benefit (~4 words)
One crisp line. Don't overload.

> *"For zero money down."*

That's it. Don't explain. Don't elaborate yet.

### 4. Proof (~20 words)
Real numbers from a real neighbor. Specificity builds credibility.

> *"Sean was paying $141 to SDGE. I replaced that bill — now he pays just $99 for solar and a battery backup."*

Use actual numbers from your pipeline whenever possible. Made-up numbers feel made-up. Real numbers land.

### 5. AFP — Ask for Permission (~18 words)
This is the close of your pitch. You're not asking for time, you're asking for permission.

> *"This is completely different than the other thousand guys who've knocked on your door. Do you mind if I ask you just two questions?"*

The phrase "completely different than the other thousand guys" does two things: it acknowledges their experience and separates you from it in the same breath.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="The Full 60-Word Pitch">
> *"Hey, how are you? I'm with KIN Home — I helped your neighbor Sean. We're qualifying homeowners for the new virtual utility plant for zero money down. Sean paid $141 with SDGE, I replaced that — he now pays just $99 for battery and solar. This is completely different than the other thousand guys. Do you mind if I just ask you two questions?"*
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Needs Audit Bridge">
Once they say yes to two questions, you've opened the case. Now pivot to the Needs Audit:

> *"Sean didn't do solar for years because the price, the debt, and the savings just didn't add up — what stopped you from looking at it?"*

This opens their story without interrogating them. You're borrowing Sean's objection as a permission slip for them to voice theirs.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="SDGE Education (If They Engage)">
If they want to understand why bills are so high, here's the brief version:

- San Onofre nuclear plant shut down in 2012 — we lost 2,200 megawatts of local power
- San Diego now imports power from the desert
- Delivery charges now make up ~80% of your SDGE bill
- You can't reduce delivery charges by conserving — you pay them regardless

> *"Most people try to save energy to lower their bill. But 80% of your bill is for delivery — not usage. You can turn everything off and still owe SDGE."*
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="The Solve">
> *"What we do is replace your SDGE bill entirely. Instead of paying the utility, you pay into your own system. Same concept as owning vs renting."*

Then move into the SDGE hypothetical close.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Takeaway & Booking">
If they're engaged but not committing:

> *"I want to be upfront — not every home qualifies. Let me do a quick panel check while I'm here, grab your bill, and I'll know in 90 seconds if it even makes sense for you."*

The 90-second split: roof check + bill pull + booking.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Set Expectations Before You Leave">
Two things happen at the appointment:

1. A two-question discovery conversation (not a sales pitch)
2. A system design with exact rates, a credit check, and paperwork if it makes sense

> *"My manager won't let me design anything until I've asked those two questions. It's their version of quality control."*

Setting expectations prevents no-shows and makes you look professional.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Common Responses">
**"Is this solar?"**
> *"It's part of it — but it's actually a battery backup program that works with or without panels. Can I ask you two quick questions?"*

**"You're knocking on my door for this?"**
> *"I know, weird, right? Honestly, door to door is the only way we keep costs low enough to make the numbers work. That's part of why Sean saves $42 a month. Two questions?"*

**"Not interested."**
> *"Completely fair — most people feel that way until they see the bill comparison. Mind if I just show you one number real quick before I go?"*
</Accordion>
