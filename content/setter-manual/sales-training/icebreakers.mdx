# Icebreakers

The icebreaker isn't small talk. It's a precision tool. You have about three seconds to shift from "stranger at my door" to "person I'll give two minutes to." Icebreakers do that work.

> **Rule:** Never launch into the pitch until the door is open and the energy is warm. An icebreaker earns you the right to pitch.

<Accordion title="What Icebreakers Are For">
When someone answers the door, they're already on defense. They don't know you, they didn't invite you, and their default is to close the interaction as fast as possible.

An icebreaker disrupts that pattern. It signals: *I'm not a threat, I'm a human being, and this will be worth your next 30 seconds.*

Good icebreakers are:
- **Observational** — about something you can see
- **Genuine** — not scripted-sounding
- **Brief** — one or two lines maximum
- **Warm** — not witty or clever at their expense
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Categories of Icebreakers">
### Environment / Property
Comment on something specific about their home or yard. Specificity matters — "nice yard" is forgettable, "those roses are incredible" is not.

> *"Those raised beds — are you growing tomatoes back there?"*

> *"Did you guys do that flagstone yourself or hire it out? Looks incredible."*

> *"Your driveway looks brand new — did you just seal it?"*

### Pets
If a dog is visible or barking, acknowledge it before anything else.

> *"What kind is he? I've got a golden at home — they're impossible not to love."*

> *"Don't worry, I'm a dog person. What's his name?"*

Never act afraid or annoyed by a dog. How you respond to their dog tells them more about you than your pitch does.

### Kids / Family
If there are kids around, a brief acknowledgment creates instant warmth.

> *"How old? My nephew just turned four — it's the best age."*

Don't linger. One line, then transition.

### Neighborhood / Timing
If it's obvious they just got home, were in the middle of something, or it's a nice day:

> *"Hope I caught you at a decent time — I'll keep it short."*

> *"Beautiful afternoon out here. You guys have a great block."*
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="The Transition">
After the icebreaker lands, move directly into the Case Open. The icebreaker should feel like a conversation, not a pause in a script.

> *[After they respond to your comment about the garden]*
> *"Nice. Hey — reason I'm stopping by, I'm with KIN Home. I helped your neighbor Sean a few weeks ago..."*

The word "Hey" is your pivot. It signals you're shifting registers from casual to purposeful without being abrupt.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="What to Avoid">
**Don't compliment their appearance.** It reads wrong.

**Don't use weather as your only icebreaker.** "Nice day out, huh?" is so generic it signals you have nothing real to say.

**Don't icebreaker and then immediately launch into a memorized pitch.** Let them respond. Even a one-second pause after they reply creates the feel of an actual exchange.

**Don't over-icebreaker.** Two minutes of small talk before you get to the point wastes their time and yours. One or two genuine lines is plenty.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Reps to Do">
The best way to develop icebreakers is to walk a street and notice things — not to memorize lines. Train your eye before you train your mouth.

Practice this: walk a block and narrate to yourself what's noticeable about each property. The more you observe, the more natural your icebreakers become, because they're real, not performed.
</Accordion>
