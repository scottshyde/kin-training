# Icebreakers

<Lead>The icebreaker isn't small talk. It's a precision tool. You have about three seconds to shift from "stranger at my door" to "person I'll give two minutes to." Icebreakers do that work.</Lead>

<Callout type="important" title="THE RULE">
Never launch into the pitch until the door is open and the energy is warm. An icebreaker **earns you the right** to pitch.
</Callout>

<GoldRule />

<Accordion title="What Icebreakers Are For">

When someone answers the door, they're already on defense. They don't know you, they didn't invite you, and their default is to close the interaction as fast as possible.

<KeyTerm term="Pattern Interrupt">An icebreaker disrupts the defense pattern. It signals: I'm not a threat, I'm a human being, and this will be worth your next 30 seconds.</KeyTerm>

Good icebreakers are:

<StatBlock stats={[
  { value: "OBSERVATIONAL", label: "About something you can see" },
  { value: "GENUINE", label: "Not scripted-sounding" },
  { value: "BRIEF", label: "One or two lines max" },
  { value: "WARM", label: "Not clever at their expense" }
]} />

</Accordion>

<GoldRule />

<Accordion title="Categories of Icebreakers">

### Environment / Property

Comment on something **specific** about their home or yard. Specificity matters — "nice yard" is forgettable, "those roses are incredible" is not.

<PullQuote>"Those raised beds — are you growing tomatoes back there?"</PullQuote>

<PullQuote>"Did you guys do that flagstone yourself or hire it out? Looks incredible."</PullQuote>

<PullQuote>"Your driveway looks brand new — did you just seal it?"</PullQuote>

### Pets

If a dog is visible or barking, acknowledge it **before anything else**.

<PullQuote>"What kind is he? I've got a golden at home — they're impossible not to love."</PullQuote>

<PullQuote>"Don't worry, I'm a dog person. What's his name?"</PullQuote>

<Callout type="insight">
Never act afraid or annoyed by a dog. How you respond to their dog tells them more about you than your pitch does.
</Callout>

### Kids / Family

If there are kids around, a brief acknowledgment creates instant warmth.

<PullQuote>"How old? My nephew just turned four — it's the best age."</PullQuote>

<Callout type="tip">
Don't linger. One line, then transition.
</Callout>

### Neighborhood / Timing

<PullQuote>"Hope I caught you at a decent time — I'll keep it short."</PullQuote>

<PullQuote>"Beautiful afternoon out here. You guys have a great block."</PullQuote>

</Accordion>

<GoldRule />

<Accordion title="The Transition">

After the icebreaker lands, move directly into the Case Open. The icebreaker should feel like a conversation, not a pause in a script.

<PullQuote>"Nice. Hey — reason I'm stopping by, I'm with KIN Home. I helped your neighbor Sean a few weeks ago..."</PullQuote>

<KeyTerm term="The Pivot Word">The word "Hey" is your pivot. It signals you're shifting registers from casual to purposeful without being abrupt.</KeyTerm>

</Accordion>

<GoldRule />

<Accordion title="What to Avoid">

<Steps>
<Step number={1} title="Don't Compliment Their Appearance">
It reads wrong at the door.
</Step>

<Step number={2} title="Don't Default to Weather">
"Nice day out, huh?" is so generic it signals you have nothing real to say.
</Step>

<Step number={3} title="Don't Launch Into the Pitch">
Let them respond. Even a one-second pause after they reply creates the feel of an actual exchange.
</Step>

<Step number={4} title="Don't Over-Icebreaker">
Two minutes of small talk before you get to the point wastes their time and yours. **One or two genuine lines is plenty.**
</Step>
</Steps>

</Accordion>

<GoldRule />

<Accordion title="How to Practice">

<Callout type="tip">
The best way to develop icebreakers is to **walk a street and notice things** — not to memorize lines. Train your eye before you train your mouth.
</Callout>

Walk a block and narrate to yourself what's noticeable about each property. The more you observe, the more natural your icebreakers become, because they're **real, not performed**.

</Accordion>
