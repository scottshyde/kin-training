# Knocking an Install

One completed installation in a neighborhood is worth more than cold territory. A happy customer is a walking testimonial, a social proof anchor, and a referral map waiting to be activated. This technique is advanced — don't attempt it until you've mastered the Needs Audit and the Appointment Solidification sequence.

<Accordion title="Step 1: Start with the Customer">
Before you knock any neighbors, knock the install customer. The goal is to harvest social capital, not just referral names.

Open with a genuine check-in:

> *"Hey [Name] — Dave, from KIN. Just wanted to swing by and make sure everything's been smooth since the install. How's the system been treating you?"*

Let them talk. Let them complain if they need to. This isn't a pitch moment — it's relationship maintenance.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Step 2: Gather Testimonial Material">
Once they're in a positive place, ask:

> *"What are two or three of your favorite parts about the experience so far?"*

Listen closely. Whatever they say becomes your social proof at the neighbor's door:

> *"[Customer name] down the street just had theirs installed — he told me the thing he loves most is [what they said]."*

Then push for the peak:

> *"And what do you love most about the system overall?"*

The "most" question gets you the headline. Use it.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Step 3: Harvest Referrals">
> *"Since you've had it installed, who in the neighborhood has asked you about the project?"*

Wait. Let them think. Don't rush.

Most installed customers have had at least one neighbor comment or ask. These are warm referrals — people who already have curiosity and social connection.

> *"Can you point them out on this map?"*

*(Have your territory map on your phone or iPad.)*
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Step 4: Get Permission">
Before you leave:

> *"Do you mind if I knock on their door and let them know you had a great experience? I won't say anything you wouldn't say yourself — I'll just tell them you're happy with it and see if they're curious."*

The explicit permission does two things: it protects the relationship with your customer, and it gives you a social proof opener that almost always results in the door opening wide.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="The Neighbor Script">
When you knock the referral neighbor:

> *"Hey — I'm Dave with KIN Home. I was just next door with [Customer Name] — they just had a system installed and I was checking in on them. [Customer] mentioned you'd asked about it."*

*(Pause — let them respond.)*

> *"I figured I'd swing by real quick while I was in the neighborhood. They said they couldn't be happier with how it went. Do you mind if I ask you two questions?"*

The script works because:
1. It's completely honest
2. The neighbor's name is the entire Case Open
3. The curiosity and social warmth are already there
4. You're not cold — you're connected
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="What Not to Do">
**Don't knock before you've harvested.** Get the referral names and permission first. Showing up at a neighbor's door saying "your neighbor [name] said to come by" without that permission is a violation of trust.

**Don't ask for referrals before checking in on the customer.** If you show up and the customer isn't happy, this whole sequence falls apart — and now you've damaged a relationship. Check first.

**Don't over-ask.** Getting 2-3 strong referral names is better than 10 weak ones. Quality over quantity.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Why This Works">
Door-to-door cold approach closes at a fraction of the rate of referral-activated approach. When a neighbor's name opens the door, you're not a stranger — you're a person their neighbor trusts. That changes everything about the conversation before you say a word.

One install, properly worked, can seed an entire block.
</Accordion>
