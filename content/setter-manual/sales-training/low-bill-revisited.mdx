# Low Bill — The Full Flow

A low bill is one of the most common conversation stoppers for new reps. The prospect says their bill is $60 a month and the rep mentally writes off the lead. That's a mistake.

A low bill means fewer panels — not no deal. It means a conservative energy user who has more room to grow into their system than they realize.

---

## The Opening Question

When you sense or discover their bill is low, lead with:

> *"Are you concerned you'd be paying more with solar than you are now?"*

This names the objection before they do. It shows you've had this conversation before, you're not surprised, and you have something to say about it.

---

## Why Low Bill ≠ No Deal

A lower bill means a smaller system — typically 8 to 10 panels instead of 25 to 30. That system is less expensive to finance and produces enough power to cover their current usage.

> *"The beauty of a low bill is that you actually need fewer panels. A lot of people in your situation end up with a smaller system that costs less per month than what you're paying SDGE right now."*

---

## The Conservative Energy Use Question

> *"Do you conserve energy pretty aggressively between 4pm and 9pm?"*

Most low-bill households do — they avoid running laundry, dishwashers, and high-draw appliances during peak hours to manage their TOU costs.

Follow with:

> *"If it didn't cost you more — if your energy cost was the same or less — what would you use more freely?"*

This opens the conversation about latent demand. They've been holding back. Solar frees them to use energy normally, and in many cases, to expand usage (EV charging, upgraded HVAC, etc.) without increasing cost.

---

## The Relationship-Building Sequence

Once you've addressed the low bill concern, use this sequence to deepen the conversation:

**Name exchange:**
> *"I'm [name], by the way — what's yours?"*

**Confirm the bill:**
> *"And you said your bill is under $50? Is that year-round or just in the winter?"*

**Length of ownership:**
> *"How long have you been in the home?"*

**The monopoly question:**
> *"What made you choose SDGE?"*

*(Wait for the answer — which is almost always some version of: I didn't choose them, they're the only option.)*

> *"Right — monopoly. So you've just been paying whatever they charge, with no alternative."*

**Financial benefit question:**
> *"Has SDGE ever offered you anything financial in return for being a loyal customer?"*

*(They haven't. Let that land.)*

**Future timeline:**
> *"How long are you planning to stay in the home?"*

---

## The Reframe Close

After you've gathered their context, restate their concerns and flip them:

> *"So here's what I'm hearing: your bill is low, you're conservative with energy, and you're not sure it pencils out. What I'd want to show you is that with a system sized for your current usage, the math actually works in your favor. The question isn't whether you save enough — it's whether you want to keep paying a company that's never given you anything back, or own something instead."*

Then close:

> *"What's really holding you back from at least seeing the numbers?"*

---

## The Bill Under $50 Nuance

If their bill is under $50, there are two possibilities:

1. **They're truly low users** — good candidates for a small system, especially if they're thinking about an EV or have plans to expand
2. **They have an existing solar system** — check before you pitch. If they already have solar, the conversation shifts to battery storage and true-up optimization

> *"Is that $50 after solar credits, or is that just your regular bill?"*

That one question saves a lot of wasted energy.
