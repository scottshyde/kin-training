# Maintain Control in the Needs Audit

<Lead>The needs audit is a discovery process — led by you, not the prospect. When prospects start asking detailed questions before the audit is complete, your job is the same: validate, redirect, keep moving.</Lead>

<Callout type="important" title="THE RULE">
Too much information will **kill the deal**.
</Callout>

<GoldRule />

<Accordion title="Why This Matters">

<KeyTerm term="Information at the Wrong Time">When a prospect asks "How much does a system cost?" early, and you answer with a range — they anchor to the high end, decide it's too expensive, and disengage before understanding the value.</KeyTerm>

Information given at the **wrong time** becomes an objection. Information given at the **right time** becomes a decision.

<Callout type="insight">
The needs audit sequence is designed to **build value before presenting numbers**. Disrupting that sequence by answering premature questions costs deals.
</Callout>

</Accordion>

<GoldRule />

<Accordion title="The Validate and Redirect Formula">

<Steps>
<Step number={1} title="Validate">
Acknowledge the question so they feel heard.

*"That's a great question."* — *"I'm glad you brought that up."* — *"That's actually the best part about what we do."*
</Step>

<Step number={2} title="Brief Answer">
Give **just enough** to satisfy curiosity.
</Step>

<Step number={3} title="Redirect">
Move back to where you were.

<PullQuote>"...and I'll get into that in detail in just a minute. First, I want to make sure I understand your situation so I can give you accurate numbers. So back to what you were saying about your bill..."</PullQuote>
</Step>
</Steps>

</Accordion>

<GoldRule />

<Accordion title="Common Questions and Responses">

### "How much does a system cost?"

<PullQuote>"It depends entirely on your usage and lifestyle — someone with a $400 bill needs a very different system than someone with a $100 bill. That's exactly why I need to see your numbers first. What's your average monthly bill?"</PullQuote>

### "How long do the panels last?"

<PullQuote>"Industry standard is 40 to 50 years — they're rated for 25, but most systems outlast that significantly. Happy to go into more detail when my manager comes by."</PullQuote>

### "What are the warranties?"

<PullQuote>"Industry-leading 25-year warranties on panels and workmanship — which is one of the things that sets KIN apart. I'll make sure that's covered at the appointment."</PullQuote>

</Accordion>

<GoldRule />

<Accordion title="The 2+ Buying Questions Rule">

<Callout type="warning" title="DANGER SIGNAL">
If a prospect asks **two or more buying-oriented questions** in quick succession, you're in danger of losing the sale.
</Callout>

Two or more buying questions signals high interest — which sounds good. But it also means they're mentally running through the decision **before the value is fully established**.

If you answer everything, you risk:
- Giving them too much to process
- Surfacing price before they're emotionally ready
- Letting them feel like they can decide without a sit

<PullQuote>"I love that you're thinking through all of this — these are exactly the right questions. My manager is going to want to go through these with you in person, because the answers change depending on your specific setup. Let's make sure we get them out here. What does your schedule look like this week?"</PullQuote>

</Accordion>

<GoldRule />

<Accordion title="Redirect to the Appointment">

<Callout type="tip">
The needs audit has **one job**: qualify the lead and book the sit. Every question that pulls you away is a detour.
</Callout>

<PullQuote>"My manager is the expert on all of these details, and they're going to lay it all out with your actual numbers. I just want to make sure I set you up with someone who can actually answer your questions properly. When works for you?"</PullQuote>

<KeyTerm term="The Best Answer">The best answer to a detailed question is often: "Let me get the right person in front of you."</KeyTerm>

</Accordion>
