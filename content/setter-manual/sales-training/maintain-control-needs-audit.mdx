# Maintain Control in the Needs Audit

The needs audit is a discovery process — led by you, not the prospect. When prospects start asking detailed questions before the audit is complete, your job is the same: validate the question, redirect the conversation, and keep moving.

> **Rule:** Too much information will kill the deal.

<Accordion title="Why This Matters">
When a prospect asks "How much does a system cost?" early in the conversation, a rep without a protocol will answer. They'll give a range. The prospect will anchor to the high end, decide it's too expensive, and disengage — before they've ever understood the value proposition.

Information given at the wrong time becomes an objection. Information given at the right time becomes a decision.

The needs audit sequence is designed to build value before presenting numbers. Disrupting that sequence by answering premature questions costs deals.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="The Validate and Redirect Formula">
Every time a prospect asks a question before you're ready to answer it, use this three-part formula:

1. **Validate** — acknowledge the question so they feel heard
2. **Brief answer** — give just enough to satisfy curiosity
3. **Redirect** — move back to where you were

**Validate phrases:**
> *"That's a great question."*

> *"I'm glad you brought that up."*

> *"That's actually the best part about what we do."*

Then answer briefly, and redirect:

> *"...and I'll get into that in detail in just a minute. First, I want to make sure I understand your situation so I can give you accurate numbers. So back to what you were saying about your bill..."*
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Common Questions and Responses">
### "How much does a system cost?"
> *"It depends entirely on your usage and lifestyle — someone with a $400 bill needs a very different system than someone with a $100 bill. That's exactly why I need to see your numbers first. What's your average monthly bill?"*

### "How long do the panels last?"
> *"Industry standard is 40 to 50 years — they're rated for 25, but most systems outlast that significantly. Happy to go into more detail on that when my manager comes by."*

### "What are the warranties?"
> *"Industry-leading 25-year warranties on panels and workmanship — which is one of the things that sets KIN apart. I'll make sure that's covered at the appointment."*
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="The 2+ Buying Questions Rule">
This one matters: **if a prospect asks two or more buying-oriented questions in quick succession, you're in danger of losing the sale.**

Two or more buying questions signals high interest — which sounds good. But it also means they're mentally running through the decision before the value is fully established. If you answer everything they're asking, you risk:

- Giving them too much to process
- Surfacing price before they're emotionally ready for it
- Letting them feel like they've already done the analysis (and can decide without a sit)

**When this happens:**
> *"I love that you're thinking through all of this — these are exactly the right questions. My manager is going to want to go through these with you in person, because the answers change depending on your specific setup. Let's make sure we get them out here. What does your schedule look like this week?"*
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Redirect to the Appointment">
The needs audit has one job: qualify the lead and book the sit. Every question that pulls you away from that is a detour. You don't need to answer everything — you need to answer enough to keep them curious and confident, then book.

> *"Here's what I'll tell you: my manager is the expert on all of these details, and they're going to lay it all out with your actual numbers. I just want to make sure I set you up with someone who can actually answer your questions properly. When works for you?"*

The best answer to a detailed question is often: "Let me get the right person in front of you."
</Accordion>
