# The Needs Audit

<Lead>The Needs Audit is the keystone of the Griffin Hill system. This is what separates a good closer from an exceptional one — the closer who gets the business the first time versus the one who has to keep going back.</Lead>

<PullQuote attribution="Chris Voss, Never Split the Difference">A good negotiator prepares, going in, to be ready for possible surprises; a great negotiator aims to use their skills to reveal the surprises they're certain to find.</PullQuote>

<GoldRule />

<Accordion title="The Mindset">

Go in with a mindset of **discovery**. Your job here is not to present. Your job is to learn.

<Callout type="important" title="THE CORE QUESTION">
Ask yourself: **How can I get the customer to solve their own problem?**
</Callout>

<PullQuote attribution="Never Split the Difference">Negotiation is not an act of battle — it's a process of discovery. The goal is to discover as much information as possible.</PullQuote>

<Callout type="warning">
Most reps engage in **selective listening** — hearing only what they want so they can jump to their solution. That is the enemy of a good Needs Audit. Quiet your mind and actually listen.
</Callout>

</Accordion>

<GoldRule />

<Accordion title="The Five Principles">

<Steps>
<Step number={1} title="Active Listening">
- Ask questions
- Use drill-down / elaborate questions
- Validate what they say
- Use their **name** often
- Reframe and mirror statements back

<PullQuote attribution="Never Split the Difference">By listening intensely, a negotiator demonstrates empathy and shows a sincere desire to better understand what the other side is experiencing. When individuals feel listened to, they tend to listen to themselves more carefully... they become less defensive and more willing to listen to other points of view.</PullQuote>
</Step>

<Step number={2} title="Open-Ended Questions">
- Process of discovery
- Get the customer to **self-solve**
- Ask questions that drive them to their own conclusions

<KeyTerm term="OPEC Framework">Opportunity, Problems, Effects, Consequences — the four pillars of needs-based questioning.</KeyTerm>
</Step>

<Step number={3} title="Sharing Builds Trust">
- Research shows the first objection is **rarely** the real objection
- People buy from people they like and trust
- Your job is to discover as much information as possible
</Step>

<Step number={4} title="Purposeful Face-Time">
- Quality conversations over quantity
- The more time you spend with them, the **higher your close rate**
- Every minute is an investment — move them from the door to the panel, from the panel into the house
</Step>

<Step number={5} title="Position — Don't Sell">
- This is **not** the time to discuss your solution
- Create anticipation

<Callout type="tip">
No Whac-A-Mole: don't just react to whatever surfaces. Guide the conversation.
</Callout>
</Step>
</Steps>

</Accordion>

<GoldRule />

<Accordion title="Handling Objections Inside the Needs Audit">

<Callout type="insight">
When an objection surfaces during the Needs Audit, the goal is not to overcome it — it's to **understand it fully**, then continue discovering.
</Callout>

### If You Don't Know What to Say

<PullQuote>That's exactly why I'm here — other than that, what else has held you back from battery + solar?</PullQuote>

<PullQuote>Thanks for bringing that up, that's something our energy engineers handle — other than that, what else has held you back?</PullQuote>

### Low Bill (Round 1)

<PullQuote>I hear you. Are you concerned that if you went solar you'd be paying more than what you currently pay? What else?</PullQuote>

<Callout type="tip">
Variations: "We're not using much power" / "It's just the two of us" / "It's too expensive" / "The cost" / "Doesn't pencil" / "Not enough savings"
</Callout>

### Low Bill (Round 2) — They're comfortable or say no to Round 1

<PullQuote>I'm with you. Most of my clients don't go solar to save 10-20 bucks. They do it because we completely eliminate their bill. What else has held you back from going solar?</PullQuote>

### "I'm Moving"

<PullQuote>Awesome, where you guys headed? Are you moving in the next few months or in the next year or two? I get it. Are you concerned that if you go solar you'll be investing money into something that isn't your forever home?</PullQuote>

- If **yes**: *"I'm with you. Other than that, is there anything else that's held you back?"*
- If **no**: *"I understand. It sounds like your main concern is that if you move it could be a hassle to sell the house. Right? Other than moving, is there anything else?"*

### "We Need a New Roof"

<PullQuote>I'm with you. Do you have any leaks right now?... How long have you had it?... Have you received any roof quotes?... What's been holding you back from getting a new roof?</PullQuote>

<Callout type="insight">
When they say "We don't have the money" for the roof: *"I understand — not a lot of people have 20-30k just laying around to pay cash for a roof. Other than being concerned about the roof, is there anything else that's held you back from going solar?"*
</Callout>

### "Not a Priority Right Now"

<PullQuote>I get it. Are you worried you'd have to take money away from your current projects in order to pay for solar?</PullQuote>

### "We Have So Many Projects Going On"

<PullQuote>I'm with you. It sounds like to me that solar is something you want to do but you feel like you'd be spreading yourself too thin — right? Aside from that, is there anything else that's held you back?</PullQuote>

### "I've Heard Bad Things About Solar"

<PullQuote>Of course. What are some of the bad things you've heard? What else? What else? I totally get it — what are some good things you've heard?</PullQuote>

### "I'm Going to Be Dead in 10 Years"

<PullQuote>Yeah, I'm really glad you brought that up. Come on — you've got a lot longer than that. Well... let's play that hypothetical out. Say you weren't here in 10 years and you already had the solar. What's the situation you want to avoid? How would you want that to play out?</PullQuote>

</Accordion>

<GoldRule />

<Accordion title="OPEC Example Questions">

<Callout type="important" title="THE FRAMEWORK">
Every question in your Needs Audit should fall into one of four categories. These aren't random — they follow a sequence. Start with Opportunity to understand where they are, surface Problems they're living with, explore the Effects those problems create, and land on the Consequences of doing nothing.
</Callout>

### O — Opportunity Questions
*Understand their current situation and where the gap is.*

<PullQuote>"How much is your electric bill running you in the summer months?"</PullQuote>

<PullQuote>"How long have you guys been in the home?"</PullQuote>

<PullQuote>"What have you heard about solar so far — from neighbors, friends, or anyone who's looked into it?"</PullQuote>

<PullQuote>"Do you and your spouse make decisions on things like this together?"</PullQuote>

<PullQuote>"Are you guys planning on staying in this home long term?"</PullQuote>

<PullQuote>"What does your electricity usage look like — are you running the AC a lot in the summer?"</PullQuote>

### P — Problem Questions
*Surface the frustrations, pain points, and friction they're already feeling.*

<PullQuote>"What frustrates you the most about your electricity bill?"</PullQuote>

<PullQuote>"Have you noticed your rates going up year over year?"</PullQuote>

<PullQuote>"Do you ever feel like no matter what you do — turning off lights, adjusting the thermostat — the bill just keeps climbing?"</PullQuote>

<PullQuote>"Have you heard anything about solar from neighbors or friends that made you hesitant? What did they tell you?"</PullQuote>

<PullQuote>"Is there anything about your current energy setup that you wish was different?"</PullQuote>

<PullQuote>"When you see that summer bill hit, what goes through your mind?"</PullQuote>

### E — Effect Questions
*Make them feel the weight of the problem — how does it actually impact their life?*

<PullQuote>"When the bill spikes in the summer, does that change how you guys use your home? Are you keeping the house warmer than you'd like?"</PullQuote>

<PullQuote>"How does that $300-400 bill affect the rest of your monthly budget?"</PullQuote>

<PullQuote>"If rates keep going up 6-8% a year — on a $250 bill that's an extra $20 a month this year, then another $22 next year, then another $24 the year after that — it just keeps stacking. What does that look like for you guys three or four years from now?"</PullQuote>

<PullQuote>"Does that uncertainty — not knowing what the bill is going to be — stress you out at all?"</PullQuote>

<PullQuote>"Has the cost of electricity ever stopped you from doing something you wanted — running the pool heater, keeping the AC at 72, anything like that?"</PullQuote>

<PullQuote>"When you hear those kinds of stories from other people, what part of it concerns you the most about your own situation?"</PullQuote>

### C — Consequence Questions
*What happens if they do nothing? Make the status quo feel expensive.*

<PullQuote>"If nothing changes and you keep paying SDG&E at the rate they're raising prices — on a $250 bill, that's roughly $50,000 over the next 10 years, and it grows every single year because the increases compound. What does that number feel like when you hear it out loud?"</PullQuote>

<PullQuote>"What happens when your kids are older, running more electronics, more AC — and the rates are double what they are today?"</PullQuote>

<PullQuote>"If you don't lock in a rate now, and rates jump another 8% next year — that's roughly an extra $20-25 a month on top of what you're already paying, and that increase never goes back down — where does that leave you?"</PullQuote>

<PullQuote>"Your neighbors who went solar last year are already locked in. Every rate increase from here on out — they don't feel it. You do. Does that sit right with you?"</PullQuote>

<PullQuote>"If you're planning on being here long term, what does it cost you to wait? Every month you're paying SDG&E full price is a month you're not saving."</PullQuote>

<PullQuote>"Let's say you don't do anything today. Where does that leave you a year from now? Same bill, higher rates, same frustration?"</PullQuote>

<Callout type="tip" title="THE SEQUENCE MATTERS">
Don't jump to Consequences before you've built through Opportunity, Problems, and Effects. If you ask "what happens if you do nothing?" before they've told you what frustrates them and how it impacts their life, it feels pushy. When you follow the sequence, the Consequence question feels like a natural conclusion — because they arrived there themselves.
</Callout>

<Callout type="important" title="MAKE THE MATH REAL">
Percentages don't land — dollars do. Plug in their actual bill below and show them what staying with SDG&E really costs. Use this at the door on your iPad or before an appointment to prep your numbers.
</Callout>

<Accordion title="OPEC Questions — Elite Level">

<Callout type="important" title="THE DIFFERENCE">
Fundamental questions gather information. Elite questions change how the customer **thinks**. These use hypotheticals, reframes, and inversions to guide the prospect toward their own conclusion — the same way the SDGE Story works. You're not finding facts. You're building a world where saying yes feels obvious and saying no feels expensive. Every question here is open-ended. No yes/no exits. They have to think, articulate, and in doing so — sell themselves.
</Callout>

<KeyTerm term="The Rule">A Elite question should make the customer pause, think, and answer in a way that moves them closer to the solution — without you ever saying the word "solar." They sell themselves. You just hold the flashlight.</KeyTerm>

### O — Opportunity (Elite)
*Don't just ask where they are. Make them picture where they could be.*

<PullQuote>"If you could design your dream energy situation — exactly how YOU would want it — what would that look like?"</PullQuote>

<Callout type="insight">
This is one of the most powerful questions you can ask. Most prospects have never been invited to imagine what they actually want. Their answer tells you their values, priorities, and buying triggers — and they just handed them to you voluntarily.
</Callout>

<PullQuote>"If I could wave a magic wand and fix one thing about your energy situation tomorrow, what would you want me to fix?"</PullQuote>

<PullQuote>"When you bought this house, what was the vision? How does the electricity bill fit into that picture?"</PullQuote>

<PullQuote>"When you think about this home five years from now, what do you want to be different about how it runs?"</PullQuote>

<PullQuote>"What would energy independence actually mean for your family — like, walk me through what that looks like day to day?"</PullQuote>

### P — Problem (Elite)
*Don't just surface the pain. Reframe it so they see it differently.*

<PullQuote>"Here's what's interesting — you're already paying for a solar system. You're just paying SDG&E for theirs instead of owning your own. How does that land when you hear it out loud?"</PullQuote>

<Callout type="insight">
This reframe is borrowed from the SDGE Story playbook. You're not introducing a new cost — you're showing them they're already spending the money. The only question is who it goes to.
</Callout>

<PullQuote>"When you think about all the things in your life you have control over — your mortgage, your car payment, your groceries — what makes electricity the one thing we just hand over to someone else and say 'charge me whatever you want'?"</PullQuote>

<PullQuote>"You mentioned your bill was around $280 in the summer. If I told you that $220 of that is just delivery fees — not even the electricity itself — how does that change the way you think about what you're paying for?"</PullQuote>

<PullQuote>"What's your biggest perceived risk about making a change to how you power your home?"</PullQuote>

<Callout type="tip">
When they answer this, don't solve it immediately. Say: *"That's fair. A lot of people feel that way before they see their numbers. Tell me more about where that concern comes from."* The first answer is almost never the real one. The Elite move is going one level deeper.
</Callout>

<PullQuote>"If every person you knew who went solar told you it was the best decision they ever made — what would be the first thing you'd want to know about your own home?"</PullQuote>

### E — Effect (Elite)
*Don't just ask how the problem affects them. Make them live in the future where it never gets fixed.*

<PullQuote>"Let's say it's August 2029. Rates have gone up 6-8% every year like they have been — on a $250 bill, that's roughly an extra $20 a month every single year. So year one you're at $270, year two you're at $290, year three you're over $310. Your neighbor locked in three years ago and still pays $140. Walk me through how that feels sitting in your kitchen looking at that bill."</PullQuote>

<PullQuote>"You said you've been thinking about solar for a while. If you were going to be honest with yourself — what has that hesitation already cost you? Like, literally in dollars — what's the number?"</PullQuote>

<Callout type="insight">
This is uncomfortable — on purpose. You're not being aggressive. You're giving them permission to do math they've been avoiding. When they calculate what they've already spent by waiting, the urgency becomes self-generated.
</Callout>

<PullQuote>"If your kids grow up and ask you, 'Why didn't we go solar when everyone else on the street did?' — what do you tell them?"</PullQuote>

<PullQuote>"You mentioned you're putting money into the house. At 8% a year, your electricity bill grows by $20-30 more every single year — by the time those projects are done in a few years, you could be paying an extra $80-100 a month just in rate increases. What does that do to the return on everything you're building here?"</PullQuote>

<PullQuote>"When you're sitting in the house in July with the AC at 78 because you don't want the bill to spike — tell me, who made that decision for you? How does that feel knowing somebody else controls that?"</PullQuote>

### C — Consequence (Elite)
*Don't just ask what happens if they do nothing. Make "nothing" feel like a decision with a price tag.*

<PullQuote>"Here's what I've noticed — nobody ever says 'I wish I had waited longer to go solar.' But I hear 'I wish I'd done this sooner' on almost every install. What do you think those people mean by that?"</PullQuote>

<Callout type="insight">
You're using social proof as a question. They answer it and in doing so, they talk themselves into urgency. You didn't push — they pulled.
</Callout>

<PullQuote>"Doing nothing is a decision too — it's a decision to keep paying SDG&E's rate, whatever they decide it is, for as long as you live here. Tell me — how does that decision serve your family?"</PullQuote>

<PullQuote>"When I come back through this neighborhood in a year and your bill is $50-80 higher per month — that's an extra $600 to $960 a year just gone, and those rate increases are already approved — what do you think you'll wish you had done differently today?"</PullQuote>

<PullQuote>"You've been paying SDG&E for [X] years. In all that time, when have they ever called you and said 'Hey, we want to save you money'? So what does it tell you that we're standing here having this conversation right now?"</PullQuote>

<PullQuote>"What would need to be true for you to feel 100% confident this was the right move? Walk me through that — what does that look like for you?"</PullQuote>

<Callout type="tip" title="THE BRIDGE TO SNE">
That last question is your transition. Whatever they describe — seeing the numbers, understanding the payment, knowing the timeline — you say: *"That's exactly what the appointment covers. My energy specialist walks you through all of that for your specific home. What does Tuesday look like for you and your wife?"*
</Callout>

<PullQuote>"Let me ask you this — when you add up what you've paid SDG&E over the [X] years you've been in this house, what do you think that total number looks like? And what do you have to show for it?"</PullQuote>

<Callout type="warning" title="HOW TO USE THESE">
Elite questions are not a script you run through in order. They're tools. You pull the right one based on what the prospect just said. If they mention money — hit them with the "you're already paying for solar, you just don't own it" reframe. If they mention risk — ask "what's your perceived risk?" and go deeper. If they're stuck on timing — use the "what has hesitation already cost you?" question. Read the person. Match the question to the moment.
</Callout>

</Accordion>
