# Nonverbal Communication

Before you say a word, the prospect has already formed an impression. Nonverbal communication accounts for roughly **55% of overall impact** — more than your tone (38%) and far more than your words (7%).

This means that becoming a skilled communicator starts with your body, not your script.

---

## Creating Your Office at the Door

You don't stand wherever you end up — you position yourself deliberately. The door is your office. Set it up like one.

**Distance:** Stand 3 to 5 feet from the door. Close enough to be engaged, far enough to not feel invasive.

**Side:** Stand on the **hinge side** of the door, not the handle side. The hinge side is slightly less threatening — you're not between them and the ability to close the door.

**Angle:** Stand at a **45-degree angle**, not square-on. Square-on feels confrontational. An angle feels like a conversation rather than a standoff.

**Weight distribution:** Put your weight on your front leg. This reads as engaged and forward-leaning — interested, not waiting.

---

## Eye Contact

Eye contact communicates trust, confidence, and attention. Done wrong, it's either evasive (looks shifty) or too intense (feels predatory).

**The 3-second rule:** Hold eye contact for about 3 seconds, then break it naturally — ideally to point at something (a neighbor's house, the roof, a panel on the iPad). This creates a natural rhythm that doesn't feel like a stare-down.

**When pointing:** Look in the direction you're pointing. This invites them to look with you, which is a subtle bonding gesture.

## Smiling

Your smile is your greatest asset at the door. People are wired to respond to genuine smiles — they're literally contagious at a neurological level.

Seven things a genuine smile does:
1. Disarms defensiveness
2. Creates immediate warmth
3. Signals you're not a threat
4. Invites reciprocity
5. Makes you more attractive and credible
6. Boosts your own mood and confidence
7. Sets the emotional tone of the interaction

The operative word is **genuine**. A forced smile reads as fake and undermines everything you're trying to build. If you're not in a mental state where smiling feels natural, work on your state before you knock.

---

## Head Nods

Nodding while someone speaks communicates agreement and encouragement. It signals: *keep going, I'm with you.*

Strategic nods: **nod when you want them to agree with you.** When you're making a point you want them to accept, nod slowly as you say it. This is a subtle but documented technique for building agreement — mirroring creates alignment.

Don't over-nod. Constant nodding reads as nervous energy or people-pleasing.

---

## Reading Buying Cues

You're not just transmitting — you're receiving. Read their body constantly.

**Positive cues (keep going, they're with you):**
- Smiling or nodding along
- Direct eye contact
- Feet planted (not turned to leave)
- Relaxed face
- Disarming their iPad or phone
- Leaning slightly toward you

**Negative cues (something's off):**
- Lip biting or pursed lips (suppressing something)
- Gulping or dry swallowing (anxiety or disagreement)
- Shifting weight or crossing arms
- Fast, restless gestures
- Glancing back inside or at their phone

When you see negative cues, slow down. Ask a question. Find out what's happening before you push forward.

---

## Your Own Cues

The prospect is reading you too, whether or not they know it. Common self-sabotaging habits:

- **Talking too fast** — signals nervousness
- **Fidgeting with your iPad or phone** — signals insecurity
- **Not making eye contact** — signals lack of confidence or dishonesty
- **Crossing your own arms** — signals defensiveness
- **Looking at your notes while pitching** — breaks connection

Record yourself on video doing a mock pitch. Watch it once without sound. What does your body say?

---

## The "Disarm" Move

When a prospect is tense or guarded, a well-timed disarm can shift the energy. Options:

- Take a small step back (creates space, reduces pressure)
- Look away briefly at the property and comment genuinely on something
- Laugh at yourself if something goes wrong
- Ask a question and wait for the answer without filling the silence

Creating space — physical and verbal — often does more than any word track.
