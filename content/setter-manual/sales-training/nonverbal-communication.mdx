# Nonverbal Communication

<Lead>Before you say a word, the prospect has already formed an impression. Nonverbal communication accounts for roughly **55% of overall impact** — more than your tone (38%) and far more than your words (7%). Your body language is the foundation for all sales.</Lead>

<StatBlock stats={[
  { value: "55%", label: "Body Language Impact" },
  { value: "38%", label: "Tone & Paraverbal" },
  { value: "7%", label: "Words Themselves" }
]} />

<Callout type="important" title="THE RESEARCH">
"People make 11 essential decisions about us in the first 7 seconds of our engagement." — Michael Solomon PhD, NYU. Additional research from Princeton suggests people make a judgment of someone's trustworthiness in **the first 1/10 of a second**.
</Callout>

<KeyTerm term="The Reality">The first impression you make will last throughout your entire approach. Rarely will you get a second chance to alter it. You cannot choose to not communicate nonverbally — it occurs whether you want it to or not. If you look or sound nervous, timid, or unsure, the customer will feed you excuses or tell you they'll "call you."</KeyTerm>

<GoldRule />

<Accordion title="Creating Your Office at the Door">

<KeyTerm term="The Door Is Your Office">You don't stand wherever you end up — you position yourself deliberately. Occupying the space with a calm presence and confidence gives you control.</KeyTerm>

<Steps>
<Step number={1} title="Distance: 3-5 Feet">
Close enough to be engaged, far enough to not feel invasive. This gives you and the customer enough room to read one another's body language.
</Step>
<Step number={2} title="Side: Hinge Side">
Stand on the **hinge side**, not the handle side. Standing on the wrong side forces you to shuffle around to meet the customer — destroying the feeling of "your office" and creating awkward movement that detracts from your first impression.
</Step>
<Step number={3} title="Angle: 45 Degrees">
Do not stand square to the customer — that's a confrontational position. An angle feels like a **conversation** rather than a standoff.
</Step>
<Step number={4} title="Weight: Front Leg">
Most of your weight on the front leg. This prevents you from shuffling back and forth and reads as engaged and forward-leaning — interested, not waiting.
</Step>
<Step number={5} title="Act Busy">
Acting busy after you knock prevents someone from sending you away through the window. Look at your iPad, check notes, appear purposeful.
</Step>
</Steps>

<Callout type="insight">
Your mindset after knocking the door should be: **"My goals are bigger than your five minutes."** You cannot feel like you are inconveniencing them or they will take advantage of that and exploit it. Have confidence in what you are selling. We have the best product and give the best customer experience on the planet.
</Callout>

</Accordion>

<GoldRule />

<Accordion title="Eye Contact">

<KeyTerm term="The 3-Second Rule">Direct eye contact transmits confidence and honesty. Hold eye contact for about 3 seconds, then break naturally — ideally to point at something (a neighbor's house, the roof, the iPad). This creates a natural rhythm without a stare-down. More than 3 seconds can generate a negative first impression.</KeyTerm>

<Steps>
<Step number={1} title="Introduce Yourself — Eyes On">
Look the customer in the eye for 3 seconds while introducing yourself.
</Step>
<Step number={2} title="Break to Point">
Break eye contact to point at the neighbor you're taking care of. This invites them to look **with you** — a subtle bonding gesture.
</Step>
<Step number={3} title="Return Eye Contact">
Bring your gaze back to continue the case open.
</Step>
</Steps>

**Important moments for direct eye contact:** during a greeting, when asking questions, when a customer gives a smokescreen or concern, and when emphasizing a point.

<Callout type="tip" title="THE EXCEPTION">
Only if you sense the customer is about to reject you mid-sentence at the beginning of your case open may it be better to **not** look at them — looking gives the customer the opportunity to dismiss you.
</Callout>

<PullQuote>"We're not buying whatever you're selling." — Acknowledge with a slight smile and nod: "Sure, just a heads up..." — now breaking eye contact — "I'm with Susan right in front of you..." — now bringing eye contact back to continue the case open.</PullQuote>

<KeyTerm term="The Eyes Are the Window">Good eye contact combined with direct, open eyes communicates that you are a genuine, straightforward professional. Your eyes need to tell the customer that you genuinely care for them and that you're here to help provide a needed solution. Continually looking down or away will make you appear untrustworthy. Shifty or squinty eyes will cost you the sale.</KeyTerm>

</Accordion>

<GoldRule />

<Accordion title="Smiling">

<Callout type="important" title="YOUR GREATEST ASSET">
The secret to sales is that people buy from those they like and respect. Smiling is universally liked by all. Emotions are contagious — whether you choose to smile or show a mask of frustration, the customer will mirror you back.
</Callout>

<StatBlock stats={[
  { value: "10 SEC", label: "Window to Create a Positive Impression" },
  { value: "7 YRS", label: "Longer Lifespan for Frequent Smilers" }
]} />

<Steps>
<Step number={1} title="Shows Confidence">Projects assurance without saying a word.</Step>
<Step number={2} title="Creates a Positive First Impression">You have about ten seconds — your smile does most of the work.</Step>
<Step number={3} title="Breaks the Ice">Dissolves the stranger-at-my-door tension instantly.</Step>
<Step number={4} title="Universally Liked">Every culture, every demographic responds positively to a genuine smile.</Step>
<Step number={5} title="Breaks Down Barriers">Defensiveness softens when someone is smiling at you.</Step>
<Step number={6} title="Contagious">Neurologically wired — they will mirror you.</Step>
<Step number={7} title="Makes You More Likable and Attractive">People want to engage with someone who looks happy to be there.</Step>
</Steps>

<Callout type="warning">
Your smile should be **polite and professional**, but not timid and eager. If you look desperate, you will give up control of the conversation and the ability to negotiate. A genuine smile reads as confidence. A forced smile reads as fake and undermines everything. If smiling doesn't feel natural, work on your state before you knock.
</Callout>

</Accordion>

<GoldRule />

<Accordion title="Head Nods">

<KeyTerm term="Strategic Nodding">Head movements are contagious and send messages that direct the customer toward agreeing or disagreeing. Other than smiling, an effective mirroring method in sales is to get your customer to say yes by positively nodding your head up and down when you want the customer to respond positively.</KeyTerm>

<Steps>
<Step number={1} title="Nod When You Want Agreement">
When making a point you want accepted, nod slowly as you say it. The customer mirrors unconsciously.
</Step>
<Step number={2} title="Never Shake Your Head No">
This helps avoid any negativity being associated with your approach — even subconsciously.
</Step>
<Step number={3} title="Continuous Throughout the Process">
Getting the customer to nod their head throughout the entire sales process makes them feel positive about your product.
</Step>
</Steps>

<Callout type="tip">
Don't over-nod. Constant nodding reads as nervous energy or people-pleasing. Deliberate, purposeful nods at key moments.
</Callout>

<KeyTerm term="The Assumption Close">You need to constantly be assuming that they will accept your product. This is easily achieved through continuous head nods, affirmations, and smiles throughout the approach.</KeyTerm>

</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Good vs. Poor Nonverbal Cues">

<StatBlock stats={[
  { value: "OCCUPY", label: "The Space" },
  { value: "CLAIM", label: "Who You Are, What You Do — Or Never Own It" }
]} />

**Good nonverbal cues:**

| Cue | Why It Works |
|-----|-------------|
| Smile and nod | Warmth + agreement |
| Keep eye contact | Confidence + honesty |
| Feet planted | Stability + composure |
| Disarm (iPad in armpit or at your side) | Non-threatening, open posture |
| Relaxed face | Calm presence = trust |

**Poor nonverbal cues:**

| Cue | What It Signals |
|-----|----------------|
| Biting lip | Preparing for bad news |
| Pursed lips | Tension, withholding |
| Gulp in your throat | Nervousness |
| Shifting side to side | Insecurity, discomfort |
| Fast gestures | Anxiety, desperation |

</Accordion>

<GoldRule />

<Accordion title="Reading Their Cues">

<Callout type="insight">
You're not just transmitting — you're receiving. Read their body constantly.
</Callout>

**Positive cues (keep going):**
- Smiling or nodding along
- Direct eye contact
- Feet planted (not turned to leave)
- Relaxed face, leaning toward you
- Arms uncrossed, open posture

**Negative cues (something's off):**
- Lip biting or pursed lips
- Shifting weight, crossing arms
- Fast restless gestures
- Glancing back inside or at phone
- Turned feet (pointing away from you)

<Callout type="warning">
When you see negative cues, **slow down**. Ask a question. Find out what's happening before you push forward.
</Callout>

</Accordion>

<GoldRule />

<Accordion title="Your Own Cues — Self-Sabotage">

The prospect is reading you too. Common self-sabotaging habits:

<Steps>
<Step number={1} title="Talking Too Fast">Signals nervousness. Slow down.</Step>
<Step number={2} title="Fidgeting with iPad/Phone">Signals insecurity. Hold it still or put it away.</Step>
<Step number={3} title="No Eye Contact">Signals dishonesty. Look them in the eye.</Step>
<Step number={4} title="Crossing Your Arms">Signals defensiveness. Keep your posture open.</Step>
<Step number={5} title="Reading from Notes">Breaks connection. Know your material.</Step>
</Steps>

<Callout type="tip">
Record yourself on video doing a mock pitch. Watch it **once without sound**. What does your body say?
</Callout>

</Accordion>

<GoldRule />

<Accordion title="The 'Disarm' Move">

When a prospect is tense or guarded:

<Steps>
<Step number={1} title="Step Back">
Creates space, reduces pressure.
</Step>
<Step number={2} title="Comment on the Property">
Look away briefly. Comment genuinely on something you see.
</Step>
<Step number={3} title="Laugh at Yourself">
If something goes wrong, own it with humor.
</Step>
<Step number={4} title="Ask and Wait">
Ask a question and wait for the answer **without filling the silence**.
</Step>
</Steps>

<PullQuote>Creating space — physical and verbal — often does more than any word track.</PullQuote>

</Accordion>
