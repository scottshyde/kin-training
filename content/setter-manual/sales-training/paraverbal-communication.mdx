# Paraverbal Communication

Words are the least important part of what you communicate. Studies on human communication consistently show that **words carry roughly 7% of the message**. The rest is delivered through nonverbal cues (55%) and paraverbal signals — tone, pace, volume, and inflection (38%).

<Accordion title="Tone">
Tone is the emotional color of your voice. It communicates your internal state more accurately than your words do — people can hear whether you're nervous, rushed, bored, or genuinely interested.

**What to aim for:** Warm, calm, and unhurried. Not overly cheerful (it reads as fake), not flat (it reads as disinterested).

**Watch for:**
- Rising intonation at the end of statements (makes you sound uncertain)
- Dropping your voice at the end of sentences (can sound defeated)
- A monotone delivery (kills engagement in under 30 seconds)

**Practice:** Record yourself doing a pitch. Don't watch — just listen. What does your tone tell someone about how you feel about the conversation?
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Pace">
Pace is how fast or slow you speak. It affects how trustworthy, how intelligent, and how in-control you appear.

**Too fast:** Sounds nervous, pushy, or like you're trying to get through something before they stop you. People can feel the desperation in speed.

**Too slow:** Sounds uncertain, condescending, or like you're reading from a script.

**The right pace:** Slightly slower than conversational. Give people time to absorb what you're saying. Pause at natural breaks.

**The power pause:** After asking a question — especially the AFP — don't fill the silence. Let them answer. Rushing past a silence signals you're afraid of it.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Volume">
Volume communicates confidence and respect simultaneously.

**Too loud:** Comes across as aggressive, especially at a door. You're in their space.

**Too quiet:** Signals insecurity. If they have to strain to hear you, they'll disengage.

**The right volume:** Match the environment. On a quiet street, quieter. In a windy afternoon, project more. Adjust in real time based on where they're standing.

**Volume variation:** Don't stay at one volume level. Naturally lower your voice when sharing something important — it creates intimacy and signals: *lean in, this matters.*
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Inflection and Emphasis">
Where you place emphasis in a sentence changes the meaning entirely.

> *"We don't charge you anything upfront."*
vs.
> *"We don't charge **you** anything upfront."*
vs.
> *"We don't charge you **anything** upfront."*

Each version tells a slightly different story. Know what you're emphasizing and why.

Key rule: **emphasize the benefit, not the feature.** "Zero money down" should land with emphasis on *zero*, not *money*.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Paraverbal and Emotion">
Your voice is a direct window into your emotional state. You cannot fake a calm, confident tone if you don't feel calm and confident — the dissonance leaks.

This is why mindset work isn't optional. If you walk up to a door thinking *I hope they don't reject me*, your voice will carry that. If you walk up thinking *I genuinely have something valuable to show them*, your voice will carry that instead.

> The best paraverbal adjustment you can make is to actually believe in what you're doing.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Practical Drills">
**Record every pitch** — not to watch, but to audit your paraverbal patterns. Listen for pace, tone variation, and where you rush.

**Mirror drill** — deliver your pitch to a mirror, focusing only on your voice. Don't think about words. Think about warmth.

**Read aloud** — spend 5 minutes a day reading aloud from anything. It loosens your voice, builds natural pace awareness, and removes self-consciousness.

**Debrief after no-shows** — often a monotone or rushed pitch contributed. Identify the moment and fix the next door.
</Accordion>
