# Powerful Position Statements

A position statement is a deliberate claim about what you do, who you are, or what makes you different — stated with enough confidence that the prospect accepts it as fact before you've offered any proof.

Done well, a position statement occupies mental space. It plants a flag. It gives the prospect a frame for the rest of the conversation.

> *Definition: stake a claim, occupy a space.*

---

## Why Position Statements Work

Most homeowners have heard a dozen solar pitches. They've been told:
- "We're the best in the area"
- "We have the lowest prices"
- "We have great reviews"

These claims are so common they register as noise. A powerful position statement is specific, confident, and often counterintuitive — which is why it cuts through.

---

## Examples

**On customer experience:**
> *"We are obsessed with providing the best customer experience on the planet. Not in solar — on the planet. That's a bold claim and we mean it."*

**On pricing:**
> *"I've personally audited five solar companies in this area. We have better pricing than all of them. I can say that because I've seen their numbers."*

**On why people go solar:**
> *"The reason most of my clients decide to go solar isn't the savings — it's that they realize they're already paying for the energy. They're just paying the wrong company."*

**On margins:**
> *"Most solar companies are operating at 20-30% profit margins. We run at 10%. The difference shows up in your monthly payment."*

**On personal credibility:**
> *"I've worked at four solar companies. KIN is the only one I've seen where I actually feel good about what we're showing homeowners. That's the reason I'm here."*

---

## Structure of a Position Statement

Every good position statement has three components:

1. **The claim** — something specific and bold
2. **The reason** — why it's true (even briefly)
3. **The implication** — what it means for the prospect

> *"We have better pricing than anyone else in the area [claim] because I've actually seen what they charge [reason]. Which means when we show you numbers, you're seeing the best available [implication]."*

---

## When to Use Them

Position statements work at two key moments:

**At the case open:** When you're positioning KIN before launching into the needs audit. One strong statement early sets the frame.

**During objections:** When a prospect says "I've heard bad things about solar companies," a position statement responds without being defensive:

> *"I understand that — and honestly, some companies deserve that reputation. The reason I can say we're different isn't just because I work here — it's because [specific evidence]."*

---

## Earning the Right to Make Them

A position statement said with hesitation isn't a position — it's a wish. The rep who says "we're the best customer experience on the planet" with conviction has done the work to believe it.

Know your product. Know your competitors. Know what makes KIN genuinely different. When you know that, the statements become natural rather than rehearsed.
