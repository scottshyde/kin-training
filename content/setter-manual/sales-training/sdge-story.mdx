# The SDGE Story

<Lead>The SDGE story is the moment in the pitch where solar stops being a product and becomes an obvious decision. It's a reframe, not a feature list — and it works because it puts the prospect in a hypothetical they can't argue with.</Lead>

<Callout type="important" title="TWO VERSIONS">
There are two versions. Learn both. Deploy based on the prospect.
</Callout>

<GoldRule />

<Accordion title="Version 1 — The Original Lock-In">

<PullQuote>"Hypothetically — if SDGE called you tonight and said, 'We'll lock in your current bill at $180 a month for the next 25 years, and at the end of those 25 years we'll just stop charging you' — would you take that deal?"</PullQuote>

Almost everyone says **yes**.

<PullQuote>"That's exactly what solar does. You lock in a fixed monthly cost for 25 years, and at the end of 25 years the payment stops and you own the system outright. SDGE will never offer you that. We can."</PullQuote>

<KeyTerm term="Why It Works">You're not asking them to accept solar. You're asking them to accept a deal that's obviously better. They say yes to the hypothetical — then you reveal it's not hypothetical. It's the product.</KeyTerm>

</Accordion>

<GoldRule />

<Accordion title="Version 2 — The Gas Station Analogy">

<PullQuote>"You know how gas prices have been all over the place? Imagine if 10 years ago, you had locked in what you were paying for gas back then. Same price, every fill-up, for the next 25 years. Would you have taken that deal?"</PullQuote>

*(They'll say yes.)*

<PullQuote>"That's the concept. Except instead of gas, it's electricity. Your SDGE rate is going up 5-7% every year — just like gas did. Solar locks in what you're paying now. Ten years from now, your neighbors are paying $350 a month to SDGE. You're still paying $140."</PullQuote>

<Callout type="insight">
Gas prices are **viscerally real** to people in a way electricity bills often aren't. The gas station analogy makes the abstract concrete.
</Callout>

</Accordion>

<GoldRule />

<Accordion title="When to Use Each Version">

<StatBlock stats={[
  { value: "VERSION 1", label: "Analytical prospects, bill frustration, numbers-driven" },
  { value: "VERSION 2", label: "Visual/story-oriented, references inflation or gas prices" }
]} />

</Accordion>

<GoldRule />

<Accordion title="Setting Up the Story">

<Callout type="tip">
The story lands harder when it follows the SDGE education sequence.
</Callout>

<Steps>
<Step number={1} title="San Onofre Shutdown">
Lost local power.
</Step>
<Step number={2} title="Desert Import">
Delivery costs skyrocket.
</Step>
<Step number={3} title="80% of Your Bill Is Delivery">
Conservation doesn't help.
</Step>
<Step number={4} title="The Solve">
"So what we do is replace that system entirely..."
</Step>
</Steps>

The education creates the problem. The SDGE story presents the solve. **The sequence matters.**

</Accordion>

<GoldRule />

<Accordion title="After the Story">

<Callout type="important" title="DON'T LET IT FLOAT">
Land it immediately. The story creates desire — you close before that desire fades.
</Callout>

<PullQuote>"The only reason people don't do this is they haven't seen the numbers for their specific home. That's exactly what the appointment is for — to show you your numbers. What does Tuesday look like?"</PullQuote>

</Accordion>

<GoldRule />

<Accordion title="Including Roof in the Hypothetical">

For prospects with roof concerns, extend Version 1:

<PullQuote>"Hypothetically — what if SDGE offered you that same deal, but they also replaced your roof as part of the package? Same fixed payment, new roof, stops charging you in 25 years. Would that be worth seeing?"</PullQuote>

<KeyTerm term="The Move">This neutralizes the roof objection inside the hypothetical. They've agreed to the concept before you've raised the complexity.</KeyTerm>

</Accordion>
