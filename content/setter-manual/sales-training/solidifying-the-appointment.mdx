# Solidifying the Appointment

<Lead>Setting the appointment is not the finish line. Solidifying it is. A rep who books ten appointments a week and shows up to three has a pipeline problem — but it looks like a closing problem. The actual issue is almost always at the door: the appointment wasn't solid when they left.</Lead>

<Callout type="important" title="THE RULE">
A weak appointment is worse than no appointment. It wastes everyone's time and crushes your conversion rate.
</Callout>

<GoldRule />

<Accordion title="What Makes an Appointment Solid">

<StatBlock stats={[
  { value: "4", label: "Qualities of a Solid Appointment" }
]} />

<Steps>
<Step number={1} title="Specific Time">
Not "sometime this week" — **Tuesday at 6:30pm.**
</Step>

<Step number={2} title="Confirmed Contact Info">
Both reps and homeowners have each other's numbers.
</Step>

<Step number={3} title="Personal Rapport">
They know you and like you, not just a company.
</Step>

<Step number={4} title="Professional Rapport">
They understand what's happening at the sit.
</Step>
</Steps>

<KeyTerm term="The Standard">The appointment you're booking is a commitment from a real person. Treat it like one.</KeyTerm>

</Accordion>

<GoldRule />

<Accordion title="The Option Close">

When they're willing but haven't pinned down a time:

<PullQuote>"What works better for you — evenings during the week, or this weekend?"</PullQuote>

*(They answer — say, evenings.)*

<PullQuote>"Tuesday or Wednesday?"</PullQuote>

*(They answer — say, Tuesday.)*

<PullQuote>"Six or six-thirty?"</PullQuote>

<KeyTerm term="Why This Works">You're not asking them to find time. You're helping them choose between options. The decision feels **smaller at each step** — narrowing rather than open-ended.</KeyTerm>

</Accordion>

<GoldRule />

<Accordion title="Get Off the Doorstep">

<PullQuote>"Let me just grab your number real quick so my manager can confirm with you."</PullQuote>

<Callout type="insight">
This moves you from pitch mode to **logistics mode**, which feels less pressured and more like two people coordinating. It also gets their number into your phone — which means the confirmation text happens.
</Callout>

</Accordion>

<GoldRule />

<Accordion title="Build Both Kinds of Rapport">

<StatBlock stats={[
  { value: "PERSONAL", label: "They like you — they'd open the door again" },
  { value: "PROFESSIONAL", label: "They respect your credibility" }
]} />

**Personal rapport:**

Use their name once or twice. Ask one personal question that's not about solar. Reference something you noticed at the door (*"Hope the dog settles in after I leave"*).

**Professional rapport:**

<PullQuote>"My manager is really good at what they do — people usually appreciate how straightforward they are with the numbers. I want to make sure you're set up with someone worth your time."</PullQuote>

</Accordion>

<GoldRule />

<Accordion title="The Second Close">

If they're still wavering after the option close, go back into the needs audit:

<PullQuote>"Can I ask — what would need to be true for this to make sense for you?"</PullQuote>

or:

<PullQuote>"Everyone who was really excited about solar eventually went forward with it. What's really holding you back right now?"</PullQuote>

<KeyTerm term="Gentle But Direct">This surfaces the actual concern, which you can often address on the spot or position the appointment as the place to resolve it.</KeyTerm>

</Accordion>

<GoldRule />

<Accordion title="Confirmation Text">

<Callout type="tip" title="WITHIN 30 MINUTES">
Send a confirmation text within 30 minutes of leaving the door.
</Callout>

The text should:
- Reference the **time and date** specifically
- Include **their name**
- Sound like a **human**, not an automated system

See the **Text Templates — Setter** article for exact word tracks.

</Accordion>

<GoldRule />

<Accordion title="What a Solid Appointment Feels Like">

<Callout type="insight">
When you leave a door with a solid booking, it feels different. They know your name. You know theirs. They have a specific time in their calendar. You have their number. They said something like **"yeah, I'm actually curious about this."**
</Callout>

<KeyTerm term="The Litmus Test">That's the standard. Anything less than that is a maybe dressed up as a yes.</KeyTerm>

</Accordion>
