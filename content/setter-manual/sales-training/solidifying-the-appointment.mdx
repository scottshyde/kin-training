# Solidifying the Appointment

Setting the appointment is not the finish line. Solidifying it is. A rep who books ten appointments a week and shows up to three has a pipeline problem — but it looks like a closing problem. The actual issue is almost always at the door: the appointment wasn't solid when they left.

> **Rule:** A weak appointment is worse than no appointment. It wastes everyone's time and crushes your conversion rate.

<Accordion title="What Makes an Appointment Solid">
A solid appointment has four qualities:

1. **Specific time** — not "sometime this week," but Tuesday at 6:30pm
2. **Confirmed contact info** — both reps and homeowners have each other's numbers
3. **Personal rapport** — they know you and like you, not just a company
4. **Professional rapport** — they understand what's happening at the sit

The appointment you're booking is a commitment from a real person. Treat it like one.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="The Option Close">
When they're willing but haven't pinned down a time, use the option close:

> *"What works better for you — evenings during the week, or this weekend?"*

*(They answer — say, evenings.)*

> *"Tuesday or Wednesday?"*

*(They answer — say, Tuesday.)*

> *"Six or six-thirty?"*

This narrows the choice at each step rather than asking an open question ("When are you free?") that produces "I'll have to check" and never results in a booked time.

**Why this works:** You're not asking them to find time. You're helping them choose between options. The decision feels smaller at each step.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Get Off the Doorstep">
As soon as you have a tentative time, create a small physical transition that signals the interaction is progressing:

> *"Let me just grab your number real quick so my manager can confirm with you."*

This moves you from pitch mode to logistics mode, which feels less pressured and more like two people coordinating. It also gets their number into your phone — which means the confirmation text happens.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Build Both Kinds of Rapport">
Before you leave, you want them to feel two things:

**Personal rapport:** They like you. They'd open the door again if you knocked.

> Use their name once or twice. Ask one personal question that's not about solar. Reference something you noticed at the door ("Hope the dog settles in after I leave").

**Professional rapport:** They respect your credibility and are treating the appointment like a real thing.

> *"My manager is really good at what they do — people usually appreciate how straightforward they are with the numbers. I want to make sure you're set up with someone worth your time."*
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="The Second Close">
If they're still wavering after the option close, go back into the needs audit:

> *"Can I ask — what would need to be true for this to make sense for you?"*

or:

> *"Everyone who was really excited about solar eventually went forward with it. What's really holding you back right now?"*

This is gentle but direct. It surfaces the actual concern, which you can often address on the spot or position the appointment as the place to resolve it.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Confirmation Text">
Send a confirmation text within 30 minutes of leaving the door. The text should:
- Reference the time and date specifically
- Include their name
- Sound like a human, not an automated system

See the **Text Templates — Setter** article for exact word tracks.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="What a Solid Appointment Feels Like">
When you leave a door with a solid booking, it feels different. They know your name. You know theirs. They have a specific time in their calendar. You have their number. They said something like "yeah, I'm actually curious about this."

That's the standard. Anything less than that is a maybe dressed up as a yes.
</Accordion>
