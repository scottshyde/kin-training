# Stickier Appointments

<Lead>The difference between a 40% show rate and a 70% show rate isn't luck or territory. It's the quality of the door interaction that produced the booking. Stickier appointments come from doing four things deliberately: gathering schedule context, getting off the doorstep, building both kinds of rapport, and using the option close.</Lead>

<GoldRule />

<Accordion title="Softball Questions">

<KeyTerm term="What They Are">Softball questions gather the prospect's schedule before asking for a time. They feel like casual conversation — because they are — but they give you the intel for a precise option close.</KeyTerm>

<PullQuote>"Do you work during the week, or are you home more during the day?"</PullQuote>

<PullQuote>"Is evenings usually better for you, or do you prefer mornings on weekends?"</PullQuote>

<PullQuote>"Are you usually around in the evenings or do you have a pretty packed week?"</PullQuote>

<Steps>
<Step number={1} title="Intel for the Option Close">
They give you real schedule data to work with.
</Step>
<Step number={2} title="Make the Prospect Feel Considered">
They feel heard, not processed.
</Step>
</Steps>

<Callout type="insight">
When you know their schedule before you ask for time, the option close feels **logical** instead of pushy.
</Callout>

</Accordion>

<GoldRule />

<Accordion title="Quality Needs Audit = No Reason Not to Book">

<Callout type="important" title="THE PRINCIPLE">
A thorough needs audit does more than gather information — it creates **investment**. The more a prospect shares about their situation, the more they're emotionally committed to the conversation continuing.
</Callout>

After a complete needs audit, there's genuinely no good reason not to book. They've already invested the time. They've already told you their concerns. The appointment is just the natural next step.

<KeyTerm term="Go Deeper">If the needs audit feels rushed or surface-level, the prospect hasn't invested enough to show. Ask more. Let them talk.</KeyTerm>

</Accordion>

<GoldRule />

<Accordion title="The Option Close">

Once you have schedule context, the option close is how you book without asking a wide-open question:

<PullQuote>"What works better — evenings this week, or this weekend?"</PullQuote>

*(Evenings.)*

<PullQuote>"Tuesday or Wednesday?"</PullQuote>

*(Tuesday.)*

<PullQuote>"Six or six-thirty?"</PullQuote>

*(Six-thirty.)*

<PullQuote>"Perfect. Let me get your number so my manager can confirm."</PullQuote>

<KeyTerm term="The Narrowing">At each step, the choice narrows. By the time you're at "six or six-thirty" they're already mentally there — it's just the time that's in question.</KeyTerm>

</Accordion>

<GoldRule />

<Accordion title="Getting Off the Doorstep">

<Callout type="insight" title="WHY IT MATTERS">
The doorstep is a liminal space — they're still in "decision mode" as long as you're both standing there. Getting off the doorstep moves the interaction from **"should I do this?"** to **"okay, this is happening."**
</Callout>

<Steps>
<Step number={1} title="Panel Check">
*"Let me just take a quick look at the roofline while I'm here."*
</Step>
<Step number={2} title="Phone Transition">
Pull out your phone to take down their number.
</Step>
<Step number={3} title="Step Inside">
Ask if they want to step inside while you're taking notes.
</Step>
<Step number={4} title="Walk the Property">
Walk them to the property line to look at the roof angle together.
</Step>
</Steps>

<KeyTerm term="Forward Momentum">Any small movement breaks the static energy of the doorstep interaction and signals progress.</KeyTerm>

</Accordion>

<GoldRule />

<Accordion title="Close Again with the Calendar">

<PullQuote>"So Tuesday at six-thirty — that works for you?"</PullQuote>

<Callout type="warning">
Wait for the **verbal yes.** Don't assume. The verbal confirmation matters because it activates commitment. People follow through on things they've said out loud more reliably than things that are just understood.
</Callout>

</Accordion>

<GoldRule />

<Accordion title="The Re-Entry Needs Audit">

If they've gone through everything and still won't commit to a time, re-enter the needs audit:

<PullQuote>"Can I ask you something honestly? Everyone who's been excited about this has eventually moved forward. What's really holding you back from at least seeing the numbers?"</PullQuote>

<Steps>
<Step number={1} title="Normalizes Moving Forward">
"Everyone does it" removes the feeling of being alone in the decision.
</Step>
<Step number={2} title="Names the Real Barrier">
Forces them to articulate what's actually stopping them.
</Step>
</Steps>

<KeyTerm term="The Truth">Whatever they say, address it directly — and then ask for the booking again. Most of the time, the real barrier is smaller than the one they've been performing.</KeyTerm>

</Accordion>
