# Types of Questions

Questions are the primary tool of a skilled rep. Not features, not word tracks, not objection scripts — questions. The rep who asks better questions wins more often than the rep who has the best answers. There are four types of questions, each serving a different purpose.

<Accordion title="1. Close-Ended Questions">
**What they do:** Get a clear yes or no, or a specific factual answer. They confirm, clarify, and move the conversation forward.

**When to use them:** When you need certainty, when you're wrapping up a point, or when you need to confirm shared understanding.

**Examples:**
> *"Do you currently have solar?"*

> *"Is your bill under $50 a month?"*

> *"Are you the homeowner?"*

> *"Did you get a chance to pull up your bill before I got here?"*

**The trap:** Over-relying on close-ended questions turns a needs audit into an interrogation. If every question has a yes/no answer, you're gathering data — you're not building a relationship or discovery.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="2. Open-Ended Questions">
**What they do:** Invite the prospect to share, elaborate, and think. They open the conversation rather than close it.

**When to use them:** During the needs audit and discovery phase. Any time you want to understand their situation better than a binary answer can tell you.

**Examples:**
> *"What's been your experience with SDGE?"*

> *"What stopped you from going solar when you first heard about it?"*

> *"How does your household use energy in the evenings?"*

> *"What matters most to you when it comes to a decision like this?"*

**The trap:** Asking open-ended questions and then not listening. The point of an open question is to hear the answer — really hear it — not to wait for your turn to pitch.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="3. Teaching / Prime Open-Ended Questions">
**What they do:** Stake a position or share a belief *before* asking the open question. The statement primes their thinking so they arrive at a useful insight on their own.

This is the most advanced question type. It combines education with discovery, and when done well, it creates what feels like an epiphany for the prospect — even though you planted the seed.

**Structure:** *[Position statement] + [open question]*

**Examples:**
> *"Most people don't realize that 80% of their SDGE bill is for delivery charges — not actual energy usage. When you think about your bill, does that change how you see it?"*

> *"The reason most of my clients eventually go solar is that they realize they're already paying for the energy — just to someone else. Does that resonate with your situation?"*

> *"SDGE rates have gone up an average of 6% a year for the last decade. Given that, what do you think your bill looks like in five years if nothing changes?"*

**The trap:** Making the position statement so forceful that it sounds like a lecture or an argument. The tone should be curious and collaborative, not corrective.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="4. Dispositional Questions">
**What they do:** Gather basic information — who they are, what their situation is, what their setup looks like. These are the qualifying questions of the needs audit.

**When to use them:** Early in the audit, to establish baseline context before going deeper.

**Examples:**
> *"How long have you been in the home?"*

> *"Do you get your bill online or in the mail?"*

> *"How many people in the household?"*

> *"Do you have an electric vehicle or are you thinking about one?"*

> *"Is the roof newer, or has it been a while?"*

**The trap:** Asking too many dispositional questions in a row. It starts to feel like a form. Interweave dispositional questions with open-ended ones to keep it conversational.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Putting It Together">
A skilled needs audit moves fluidly across all four types:

1. **Dispositional** to establish context (*"How long have you been here?"*)
2. **Open-ended** to understand experience (*"What's your relationship with SDGE been like?"*)
3. **Close-ended** to confirm a point (*"So you're essentially locked in with them — no real alternative?"*)
4. **Teaching/prime** to shift perspective (*"Most people don't realize they're paying 80% in delivery charges — does that change how you think about it?"*)
5. **Drill down** to go deeper (*"Tell me more about what you mean by that."*)

No script produces this sequence automatically. It comes from listening well and choosing the right tool for what the moment needs.
</Accordion>
