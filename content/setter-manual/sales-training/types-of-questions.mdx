# Types of Questions

<Lead>Questions are the primary tool of a skilled rep. Not features, not word tracks, not objection scripts — questions. The rep who asks better questions wins more often than the rep who has the best answers.</Lead>

<GoldRule />

<Accordion title="1. Close-Ended Questions">

<KeyTerm term="Close-Ended">Get a clear yes/no or specific factual answer. They confirm, clarify, and move the conversation forward.</KeyTerm>

<PullQuote>"Do you currently have solar?"</PullQuote>

<PullQuote>"Is your bill under $50 a month?"</PullQuote>

<PullQuote>"Are you the homeowner?"</PullQuote>

<Callout type="warning">
**The Trap:** Over-relying on close-ended questions turns a needs audit into an **interrogation**. If every question has a yes/no answer, you're gathering data — not building discovery.
</Callout>

</Accordion>

<GoldRule />

<Accordion title="2. Open-Ended Questions">

<KeyTerm term="Open-Ended">Invite the prospect to share, elaborate, and think. They open the conversation rather than close it.</KeyTerm>

<PullQuote>"What's been your experience with SDGE?"</PullQuote>

<PullQuote>"What stopped you from going solar when you first heard about it?"</PullQuote>

<PullQuote>"What matters most to you when it comes to a decision like this?"</PullQuote>

<Callout type="warning">
**The Trap:** Asking open-ended questions and then **not listening**. The point is to hear the answer — really hear it — not to wait for your turn to pitch.
</Callout>

</Accordion>

<GoldRule />

<Accordion title="3. Teaching / Prime Open-Ended Questions">

<Callout type="important" title="MOST ADVANCED TYPE">
Combines education with discovery. Stakes a position *before* asking the open question. The statement primes their thinking so they arrive at a useful insight **on their own**.
</Callout>

**Structure:** *[Position statement] + [open question]*

<PullQuote>"Most people don't realize that 80% of their SDGE bill is for delivery charges — not actual energy usage. When you think about your bill, does that change how you see it?"</PullQuote>

<PullQuote>"SDGE rates have gone up an average of 6% a year for the last decade. Given that, what do you think your bill looks like in five years if nothing changes?"</PullQuote>

<Callout type="warning">
**The Trap:** Making the position statement so forceful it sounds like a lecture. The tone should be **curious and collaborative**, not corrective.
</Callout>

</Accordion>

<GoldRule />

<Accordion title="4. Dispositional Questions">

<KeyTerm term="Dispositional">Gather basic information — who they are, what their situation is. The qualifying questions of the needs audit.</KeyTerm>

<PullQuote>"How long have you been in the home?"</PullQuote>

<PullQuote>"Do you get your bill online or in the mail?"</PullQuote>

<PullQuote>"Do you have an electric vehicle or are you thinking about one?"</PullQuote>

<Callout type="warning">
**The Trap:** Asking too many in a row. It starts to feel like a form. **Interweave** with open-ended questions to keep it conversational.
</Callout>

</Accordion>

<GoldRule />

<Accordion title="Putting It Together">

<Steps>
<Step number={1} title="Dispositional">
Establish context: *"How long have you been here?"*
</Step>
<Step number={2} title="Open-Ended">
Understand experience: *"What's your relationship with SDGE been like?"*
</Step>
<Step number={3} title="Close-Ended">
Confirm a point: *"So you're essentially locked in with them — no real alternative?"*
</Step>
<Step number={4} title="Teaching/Prime">
Shift perspective: *"Most people don't realize 80% is delivery charges — does that change how you think about it?"*
</Step>
<Step number={5} title="Drill Down">
Go deeper: *"Tell me more about what you mean by that."*
</Step>
</Steps>

<Callout type="insight">
No script produces this sequence automatically. It comes from **listening well** and choosing the right tool for what the moment needs.
</Callout>

</Accordion>
