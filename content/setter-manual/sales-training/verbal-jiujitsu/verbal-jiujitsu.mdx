# Verbal Jiujitsu: Handling Resistance Without Confrontation

## What is Verbal Jiujitsu?

Verbal jiujitsu is the art of redirecting resistance without meeting it head-on.

In physical jiujitsu, you don't meet force with force. You redirect the opponent's energy to use against them. Same principle applies to verbal resistance.

When someone says "I'm not interested," you don't argue and convince. You redirect and reframe.

---

## The Core Principle: Never Argue

**The Trap:** Someone says "I'm not interested," and you argue why they SHOULD be interested.

**The Result:** They become MORE defensive.

**The Better Way:** Acknowledge their statement, ask a question that opens their mind, and redirect.

---

## The Verbal Jiujitsu Techniques

### Technique 1: The Acknowledgment + Reframe

**Situation:** "I'm not interested in solar."

**Wrong Response:** "But it saves you money! You should at least hear..."

**Right Response:** "I hear you. Most people aren't interested until they see the actual numbers. That's exactly why I'm here - just to show you quickly if it makes sense. Sound fair?"

**What's happening:**
- Acknowledge their position (don't argue)
- Reframe their lack of interest as normal ("most people")
- Reposition your purpose (educator, not pusher)
- Ask permission softly

---

### Technique 2: The Curiosity Question

**Situation:** "I already looked into solar and decided against it."

**Wrong Response:** "Well, things have changed. Technology is better now..."

**Right Response:** "Oh interesting. Tell me about that. What did you learn, and what made you decide against it?"

**What's happening:**
- Show genuine curiosity (not judgment)
- Get them talking (engagement)
- Understand their real concern (not just generic rejection)
- Open door to addressing specific concern

---

### Technique 3: The Assumption Redirect

**Situation:** "Solar is too expensive."

**Wrong Response:** "Actually, you can finance it for just $X per month..."

**Right Response:** "I get it. What does 'expensive' mean to you? Like, what would feel comfortable?"

**What's happening:**
- Acknowledge their concern
- Get specific about what "expensive" means
- Shift from arguing price to understanding budget
- Now you can actually address their real concern

---

### Technique 4: The Third-Party Reference

**Situation:** "My neighbor tried solar and wasn't happy."

**Wrong Response:** "Well, they probably went with a bad company..."

**Right Response:** "Oh, that's helpful to know. What specifically happened with them? Because that's exactly the kind of situation I help people avoid."

**What's happening:**
- Learn from their reference (valuable intel)
- Separate their neighbor's experience from your offering
- Position yourself as someone who learns from other people's mistakes
- Show you're different

---

### Technique 5: The Reflection + Question

**Situation:** "I want to think about it."

**Wrong Response:** "What's to think about? You're paying too much, solar saves you money, let's move forward..."

**Right Response:** "I totally understand wanting to think about it. What specifically do you want to think through? I might be able to help clarify something right now."

**What's happening:**
- Validate their thinking process
- Find out what they're actually uncertain about
- Address the real concern (often education, not indecision)
- Give yourself a chance to help

---

### Technique 6: The Agree + Reframe

**Situation:** "Everyone says solar is a great investment, but I'm skeptical."

**Wrong Response:** "Well, it's not everyone, it's... " [defensive]

**Right Response:** "Healthy skepticism is actually smart. You should be skeptical about big decisions. That's exactly why people like you want to see the actual numbers before committing. Right?"

**What's happening:**
- Agree with their skepticism (respect it)
- Reframe skepticism as a strength (not a weakness)
- Position your role as satisfying their skepticism
- Redirect to next step naturally

---

### Technique 7: The Hypothetical Question

**Situation:** "I don't think our roof can handle solar."

**Wrong Response:** "Most roofs can. Unless you have a really old roof..."

**Right Response:** "Fair point. If we could confirm your roof is in good shape, would that change things for you?"

**What's happening:**
- Acknowledge their concern
- Test if it's the real barrier or just an excuse
- If real barrier: Get it solved first (roof engineer)
- If excuse: Understand the actual barrier

---

## The Verbal Jiujitsu Framework

**Step 1: Acknowledge**
- "I hear you"
- "That makes sense"
- "I understand"

**Step 2: Don't Argue**
- Don't defend
- Don't convince
- Don't lecture

**Step 3: Ask a Question**
- "Tell me more about that"
- "What specifically concerns you?"
- "If we could solve that, would this work?"

**Step 4: Redirect**
- Reframe their objection
- Find the real concern
- Position yourself as helper

## Common Door Resistances & Verbal Jiujitsu Responses

### Resistance 1: "Not interested"

**Your Response:**
> "I hear you. Most people aren't interested until they see how much they could save. That's all I'm asking - two minutes to show you the numbers. Sound fair?"

[Reframe: Interest comes from information, not from initial interest]

---

### Resistance 2: "Not now, I'm busy"

**Your Response:**
> "I completely understand - I'll keep it quick. Two minutes, and if it doesn't make sense, I'm out. Fair?"

[Acknowledge their time. Respect it. Ask for brief commitment.]

---

### Resistance 3: "I'm renting"

**Your Response:**
> "Got it. So you don't own. That actually makes sense then - solar works for owners. Just out of curiosity, are you thinking about buying in the area anytime soon?"

[Acknowledge. Qualify. Plant seed for future.]

---

### Resistance 4: "Already have a quote"

**Your Response:**
> "Oh cool, you've done your homework. Who'd you talk to? And what stood out to you about them?"

[Learn from their experience. Find gaps you can fill.]

---

### Resistance 5: "I don't have time to talk"

**Your Response:**
> "Perfect, I don't need much. Just one quick question: how long have you been paying SDGE? [Wait for answer] You been in the home that whole time? [Answer] OK cool. Would Thursday afternoon work better to sit down and talk details?"

[Respect their time. Ask minimal questions. Book appointment on the fly.]

---

### Resistance 6: "Let me talk to my spouse first"

**Your Response:**
> "That's smart - this is a family decision. When's your spouse home? Maybe I can talk to both of you together Thursday when they're here?"

[Validate decision-making process. Offer to meet when both present.]

---

### Resistance 7: "I don't like salespeople"

**Your Response:**
> "I get it. Most people don't like the hard sell. That's not my style. I'm here because I help people figure out if solar actually makes sense for them. Some people it does, some it doesn't. Let me ask you a quick question..."

[Acknowledge the distrust. Reposition yourself as helper, not pusher.]

## The Tone That Makes It Work

Verbal jiujitsu only works with the right tone:

**Tone Characteristics:**
- Genuine curiosity (not faked)
- Respect for their position
- Confidence (not desperation)
- Friendliness (not pushiness)
- Patience (not rushing)

**Tone Mistakes:**
- Sarcasm (comes across mean)
- Desperation (comes across needy)
- Talking too much (comes across pushy)
- Arguing (comes across defensive)
- Rushing (comes across disrespectful)

---

## The Practice: 30 Days of Verbal Jiujitsu

**Week 1:** Learn the seven techniques above.

**Week 2:** Use at least one technique every day. Notice which ones feel natural.

**Week 3:** Combine techniques. Use multiple redirects in single conversation.

**Week 4:** Refine based on what's working. Some techniques will fit your style better.

## Key Principle

Verbal jiujitsu isn't manipulation. It's the art of conversation - of respecting someone's objection while gently exploring whether it's the real barrier or just surface resistance.

When done with authenticity and respect, it's not jiujitsu at all.

It's just good conversation.

And good conversation is how you set appointments and build relationships.

---

## Final Thought

The best closers aren't the ones who convince the hardest.

They're the ones who ask the best questions, listen the deepest, and redirect resistance without ever making it confrontational.

Master verbal jiujitsu and you'll set more appointments than you ever thought possible.

Because you won't be fighting resistance.

You'll be dancing with it.
